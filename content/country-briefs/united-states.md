## Core Hypothesis

The United States may hold a frontier-access advantage through leading AI labs, model platforms, advanced accelerator supply channels, cloud providers, research universities, venture capital, and defense or federal procurement pathways. That advantage does not automatically become broad national conversion. ## Layered Read

### Frontier Access

V0 should measure frontier access through model production, benchmark-relevant performance, accelerator access, hyperscale cloud capacity, open-model availability, university and lab ecosystems, and legal access to imported or domestic chips. The United States should not receive outcome credit for these inputs until deployment and effects are separately observed. TODO_DATA: add reviewed values for frontier-relevant models, accelerator access, cloud/model supplier availability, public research compute, and model access for firms and agencies.

### Conversion Capacity

The U.S. conversion question is whether a strong frontier ecosystem can reach the long tail of firms, public agencies, factories, hospitals, schools, infrastructure operators, and defense organizations. Adoption should be separated from workflow depth, pilot-to-scale speed, procurement maturity, data readiness, standards, and implementation talent. TODO_SOURCE: verify with U.S. Census Business Trends and Outlook Survey, OECD firm-adoption research, NIST manufacturing AI materials, OMB acquisition and governance memoranda, and sector case studies.

Compute and energy are immediate conversion constraints. V0 should distinguish announced data-center investment from energized AI-capable load, local interconnection feasibility, firm-power supply, cooling and water availability, transmission timelines, and ratepayer or siting conflict. TODO_SOURCE: verify with DOE/LBNL data-center energy report, NERC reliability assessments, PJM load forecasts, ERCOT large-load materials, Berkeley Lab queue data, utility filings, and state-level siting evidence.

### Adaptation Capacity

U.S. adaptation capacity should be measured through worker training, occupational exposure, labor mobility, unemployment and wage-insurance tools, institutional trust, civil-rights enforcement, procurement safeguards, and public-sector AI governance. A voluntary or sectoral governance architecture should be coded as a specific institutional model, not as absence of governance. TODO_SOURCE: verify with OECD and ILO labor exposure work, Department of Labor and education data, Pew surveys, OMB memoranda, NIST AI RMF, EEOC guidance, and sector regulators.

### Distribution Quality

The U.S. distribution question is whether AI benefits concentrate among hyperscalers, frontier labs, large enterprises, high-income workers, and already-rich regions, or diffuse into SMEs, local manufacturers, public services, exposed workers, and lagging regions. TODO_DATA: needs firm-size AI adoption gaps, regional adoption gaps, wage and employment effects, public-service access evidence, SME support, and AI-related concentration measures.

### Realized Outcomes

Outcome evidence should be limited to measured productivity, quality, speed, cost, service availability, scientific output, industrial capability, defense readiness, welfare, or adjustment costs. Lab experiments, pilots, investment flows, and adoption surveys are useful but should not be presented as economy-wide outcomes. ## Compute-Energy Questions

The U.S. compute-energy module should track local bottlenecks rather than national electricity totals. The relevant sequence is announced capacity, contracted capacity, interconnection progress, energized load, AI-ready load, and sustained firm-power operation. ## Measurement Traps

Do not treat frontier labs as national diffusion.

Do not treat announced data-center investment as operational compute capacity.

Do not treat enterprise AI adoption as workflow depth or productivity.

Do not treat voluntary risk frameworks as irrelevant; code their institutional role and enforcement limits.

Do not infer broad welfare gains from market capitalization, venture investment, or model benchmarks.
