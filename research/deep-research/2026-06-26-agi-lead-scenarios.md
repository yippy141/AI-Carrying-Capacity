## Strategic frame

The evidence does **not** support a single ŌĆ£AI raceŌĆØ metric. A better frame is a set of related competitions in **compute, models, adoption, integration, and deployment**. Brookings argues explicitly that the United States retains the clearest edge at the technological frontier, especially in compute scale and model performance, while China is advancing through efficiency gains, open-source diffusion, and deep integration into the real economy. That is the core reason to analyze **frontier leadership** and **conversion capacity** separately rather than treating them as the same thing. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view0Ņłéturn18view1Ņłü

For this module, **conversion capacity** can be operationalized as the ability to turn model capability into durable national advantage through energy, chips, cloud access, software ecosystems, procurement, workforce adaptation, data pipelines, security, supply chains, and organizational adoption. This interpretation is supported by BrookingsŌĆÖ multidimensional competition framework, CSETŌĆÖs argument that AI statecraft must shape deployment pathways and governance abroad, and official U.S. policy that increasingly treats AI exports, infrastructure, and global stack adoption as strategic levers in their own right. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18view1Ņłéturn26view1Ņłéturn33view1Ņłü

The scenario logic should also distinguish **frontier-sensitive domains** from **conversion-sensitive domains**. CNAS and RAND both point to cyber operations, intelligence analysis, planning, and military command functions as domains where faster frontier progress may have unusually large strategic effects. By contrast, METR and Brookings suggest that broad economic impact depends much more on context, integration, and deployment frictions: AI capabilities vary sharply by domain, and even strong coding tools did not automatically speed experienced open-source developers in an early-2025 field trial. ## Open questions and limitations

This report is designed to support **scenario analysis**, not to forecast AGI timelines. The most important uncertainties are not only technical; they are political and institutional. The literature is still thin on how Chinese central leaders would rank frontier parity versus broad diffusion under acute pressure; how effective cloud-compute restrictions would be in practice; and how quickly deployment bottlenecks in either country could relax if leaders treated them as wartime priorities. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłéturn27view6Ņłéturn19view0Ņłü

There is also a live conceptual dispute over sabotage. ŌĆ£Superintelligence StrategyŌĆØ makes sabotage and deterrence central, while critics argue that verifying intent, identifying destabilizing projects, and preventing model theft are too hard for stable deterrence to work cleanly. For product design, sabotage should therefore appear as a **contingent branch with low transparency and high uncertainty**, not as the default baseline. The strongest high-confidence conclusion is narrower and more useful: **frontier leadership matters, but what it buys depends on conversion capacity.** It matters most in security-sensitive, compute-heavy, and stack-setting contexts; it matters less for broad economic outcomes when energy, deployment, organizational absorption, and open diffusion dominate. That is the scenario-module insight most strongly supported across RAND, CSET, Brookings, CNAS, Carnegie, METR, Epoch, Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and the Chinese governance and industry sources reviewed here.
