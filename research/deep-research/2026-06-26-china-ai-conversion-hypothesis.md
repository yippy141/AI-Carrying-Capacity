# China and the AI Conversion Hypothesis

## Framing the hypothesis

The hypothesis is **partly supported, but only in a specific sense**. The evidence does **not** show that China has a blanket advantage over the United States across the full AI stack. The United States still leads on several frontier inputs that matter greatly for long-run capability: production of notable frontier models, access to the most advanced chips, overall high-end compute, and elite global talent attraction. Stanford’s 2025 AI Index found that U.S.-based institutions produced 40 notable AI models in 2024 versus 15 for China, even as Chinese models narrowed the benchmark gap. Brookings likewise argues that the United States still holds a clear lead in advanced-chip access and aggregate compute capacity. citeturn14search10turn16search9turn17search15

But if “AI conversion” means **turning AI capability into widespread real-economy deployment, industrial automation, public-service applications, and local implementation at scale**, China may well have important advantages. Since 2017, Beijing has built a policy system that explicitly treats AI as a general-purpose technology for manufacturing upgrading, public administration, science, education, health, and social governance. That orientation became more concrete in the 2024 “AI+” action, deepened in the 2025 State Council opinion on implementing “AI+,” and was embedded at the center of the national Fifteenth Five-Year Plan in 2026. citeturn0search0turn33search0turn34search3turn32search0turn32search7

The strongest case for a Chinese conversion advantage rests on six interacting features. First, China has a large and unusually complete industrial base, especially in machinery, electronics, power equipment, telecom gear, vehicles, batteries, and consumer hardware. Second, it has a denser robotics and smart-manufacturing ecosystem than the United States in several key sectors. Third, it can mobilize infrastructure—power, data centers, cloud, and industrial parks—through central-local coordination more quickly than the United States often can. Fourth, it has a large engineering and vocational pipeline that is increasingly being aligned with AI deployment, not just basic research. Fifth, digital public-service capacity is already large enough to make AI rollout in health, education, and government administration administratively feasible. Sixth, China’s governance model is designed to speed diffusion through pilot zones, industrial policy, standards, filing systems, and local experimentation. citeturn28search0turn28search4turn41search1turn27search1turn35search0turn35search1turn35search15turn17search7

At the same time, the same governance model creates serious constraints. China still faces advanced-chip bottlenecks, frontier-compute constraints, capital misallocation risks, duplication across local governments, demographic drag, social-stability pressures from labor displacement, censorship-related limits on openness, and uneven data quality. Repeated official emphasis on “high-quality datasets,” “corpora,” “standardization,” and “independent and controllable” supply chains strongly suggests that these remain real bottlenecks rather than solved problems. citeturn30search2turn16search9turn29search3turn29search10turn29search1turn38search2turn39search1turn32search7

The bottom-line analytic judgment is therefore this: **China is not clearly ahead of the United States in frontier AI capability, but it may be better positioned to convert “good enough” AI into manufacturing, robotics, infrastructure, and state-administered public services faster and more deeply in selected domains.** Whether that becomes a decisive macroeconomic advantage depends less on model benchmarks than on whether these deployments produce durable productivity gains without triggering large social or financial distortions. citeturn30search7turn17search14turn17search3turn29search13

## Strategic evolution and governance architecture

China’s current AI conversion framework begins with the **2017 State Council “New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan”**, which set a three-step strategy through 2030 and explicitly linked AI to economic upgrading, national security, social governance, and international competitiveness. That plan already combined technology development with ethics, legal rules, and industrialization. citeturn0search0

The AI agenda was then woven into broader planning instruments. The **Fourteenth Five-Year Plan** elevated AI, big data, cloud, blockchain, and industrial internet as key digital industries and called for national industry-education integration platforms in fields including AI and industrial internet. The 2022 **scene innovation** guidance document then shifted from general ambition to deployment logic: it told pilot zones and localities to use concrete application scenarios to accelerate AI commercialization in smart factories, smart supply chains, smart agriculture, urban management, and social experiments. citeturn32search2turn28search4

What changed after 2024 was not the existence of an AI strategy, but its **conversion orientation**. The 2024 government work report introduced the “人工智能+” program, explicitly pairing AI application with industrial digitalization, smart cities, digital villages, and national integrated computing infrastructure. The 2025 government work report moved from launch to diffusion, calling for continued “AI+,” broad large-model application, AI phones and computers, smart connected vehicles, and smart manufacturing equipment. In August 2025, the State Council issued a dedicated opinion on implementing “AI+,” pushing toward large-scale application in science, industry, culture, public services, social governance, and security. The 2026 government work report then deepened the agenda again: it called for “intelligent economy” as a new growth form, accelerated diffusion of intelligent terminals and agents, support for open-source communities, ultra-large-scale intelligent-computing clusters, compute-power coordination, and better AI governance. The 2026 Fifteenth Five-Year Plan formalized all of this by placing “AI+” at the center of national digital transformation and governance modernization. citeturn33search0turn33search5turn34search3turn33search1turn33search4turn32search0turn32search7

This strategic evolution is tightly linked to **“new quality productive forces”**. Central documents and official commentary increasingly frame AI not as a standalone technology sector but as the key driver of a broader shift from digitization to intelligence-led growth. The implication is important for conversion analysis: the state is not pursuing AI primarily as a race for the single best model. It is pursuing AI as a cross-sector production technology intended to rewire factories, scientific research, consumption devices, public services, and urban governance. Brookings makes a similar observation from outside China, noting that Chinese policymakers are using a full-stack strategy aimed less at abstract AGI leadership than at rapid applied deployment. citeturn16search0turn34search8turn30search7

China’s governance architecture also matters. Unlike the United States, which still lacks a single binding federal AI law across the economy, China has assembled a layered regulatory stack: the **Data Security Law** and **Personal Information Protection Law** in 2021; **algorithm recommendation regulations** in 2022; **deep synthesis regulations** in 2022; the **Interim Measures for Generative AI Services** in 2023; the **National AI Industry Comprehensive Standardization System Construction Guide** in 2024; and published **AI safety governance frameworks** in 2024 and 2025. Providers of public-facing generative AI services with “public opinion or social mobilization” characteristics are channeled through filing, security, and disclosure procedures. citeturn43search7turn43search3turn43search1turn43search2turn43search0turn39search1turn19search10turn19search0

This system has a dual effect on conversion. It can **slow or narrow open consumer deployment** in sensitive domains, but it can also **reduce regulatory uncertainty for approved application classes**, especially in enterprise, industrial, and government use cases. Carnegie’s analysis of how Chinese AI regulations are made suggests that Beijing has repeatedly adjusted draft rules to avoid crushing the domestic industry, even while keeping tight control over politically sensitive uses. In other words, Chinese regulation is not simply restrictive; it is selective and channeling. citeturn17search5turn43search4turn43search5turn43search6

## Conversion engines in the real economy

The clearest evidence for a Chinese conversion advantage is in **manufacturing, robotics, and industrial systems integration**. China’s strength here is not merely “having factories.” It is the combination of a massive installed base, dense supplier ecosystems, state incentives for automation, and a policy apparatus oriented toward scaling applied AI in physical production. By 2023, China’s industrial robot density had reached **470 robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers**, compared with **295** in the United States; China had moved into the global top tier of factory automation within just a few years. CSIS further notes that China now produces **57 percent** of the industrial robots used in its market domestically, a major rise from a decade ago. citeturn41search1turn42search0turn17search2

This matters because AI conversion is easier where AI is entering already-digitized and already-automating workflows. China’s industrial-internet and smart-manufacturing policies have been pushing in that direction for years. The 2021 smart-manufacturing and digital-economy planning cycle, the 2022 scene-innovation guidance, and local “AI+ manufacturing” plans in Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, Hangzhou, and Jiangsu all aim to insert AI into design, simulation, process control, visual inspection, predictive maintenance, logistics, and industrial agents. Shanghai’s 2026–2028 advanced-manufacturing action plan, for example, explicitly calls for “AI+ manufacturing,” industry models, and industrial agents, while Beijing’s 2024–2025 “AI+” action plan was built around accelerated sector adoption. citeturn32search2turn28search4turn22search2turn40search1

The **robotics ecosystem** is especially important because it links AI software to embodied deployment. MIIT’s 2023 **Guiding Opinions on the Innovative Development of Humanoid Robots** framed humanoids as a disruptive product class combining AI, high-end manufacturing, and new materials, explicitly tying the field to new quality productive forces and the modernization of the industrial system. RAND’s 2025 work on converging robotics and frontier AI sees China as treating humanoids as a key arena of technological competition, while recent Shanghai and Shenzhen policies increasingly connect large models, embodied intelligence, and terminal manufacturing. citeturn21view1turn17search19turn10search11turn10search15

The second conversion engine is **compute and energy infrastructure**. China’s “East Data, West Computing” strategy and subsequent implementation opinions were designed to route non-real-time workloads toward western and northern regions with cheaper land, cooler climates, and better renewable-energy conditions, while keeping latency-sensitive demand closer to the east-coast consumption centers. Official policy also increasingly emphasizes “算电协同,” or compute-power coordination, between data centers and the grid. citeturn21view0turn27search4turn27search5

This does not mean China has solved the power problem. Quite the opposite: the National Energy Administration said that in 2025 China had already built **42 intelligent-computing clusters with at least 10,000 accelerator cards**, and that national computing-center electricity consumption reached **170 billion kWh**, about **1.6 percent** of total national electricity use. It projects annual incremental growth in compute-related electricity demand of more than **100 billion kWh** during the Fifteenth Five-Year Plan period and around **800 billion kWh** by 2030, or roughly **6 percent** of total electricity demand. The 2024 green data center action plan also imposed targets on rack utilization, PUE, and renewable use precisely because the energy burden is rising so quickly. citeturn27search1turn27search0

Still, on a comparative basis China may face a **less binding infrastructure bottleneck than the United States**. RAND argues that China’s AI sector benefits from faster power-system expansion and the ability to pair new computing hubs with renewables and large-scale grid investments. By contrast, Rhodium’s U.S. energy scenarios show that under high-growth assumptions, U.S. data-center demand could reach **14 percent of total power generation by 2030** and **18 percent by 2035**, underscoring how severe the American siting, permitting, and grid bottleneck may become. China’s own power challenge is substantial, but its state-building capacity in generation, transmission, and industrial siting appears stronger. citeturn27search2turn17search4turn17search12

The third engine is **standardization plus regulated deployment**. China’s 2024 national AI standardization guide covers the AI stack from infrastructure, frameworks, and models to applications, safety, and evaluation. This is bureaucratic, but it is also a conversion tool: standards, filing norms, and conformity procedures lower barriers for government procurement, SOE adoption, industrial integration, and cross-region scaling. In a fragmented and risk-averse system, the existence of a recognized standard can accelerate diffusion. Carnegie’s recent work on Chinese AI safety and regulation suggests that Beijing is unusually likely to turn nonbinding governance frameworks into practical rules and standards quickly. citeturn39search1turn39search4turn17search17

## Talent, public-service capacity, and local implementation

China’s labor-market picture is mixed for frontier AI but stronger for deployment. On the one hand, China still has a very large labor force and education system: the official statistical authorities reported more than **857 million** people aged 16–59 at the end of 2024, even as aging deepens; the Fourteenth Five-Year Plan also called for national industry-education platforms in AI and industrial internet. The state has been extending AI-related training beyond elite universities into K–12, vocational education, skills upgrading, and lifelong digital literacy. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security has launched targeted AI skills-upgrading actions, and digital-skills policy has been in place since 2021. citeturn29search9turn32search2turn15search2turn15search6

On the other hand, China’s talent advantage is more obvious in **engineering scale and applied diffusion** than in unmatched leadership at the very top of the global research hierarchy. The U.S. lead in notable frontier models and advanced compute suggests that the American ecosystem still converts top-end research talent into frontier capability more effectively. China’s comparative edge is the size of the deployable engineering workforce and the state’s ability to align universities, vocational institutions, and firms around industrial priorities. citeturn14search10turn16search9turn17search15

China’s **public-service capacity** may be underappreciated in U.S.-China AI comparisons. The country already operates digital systems at a scale that makes AI insertion practically meaningful: the National Smart Education Public Service Platform had more than **178 million users** and cumulative access above **72.6 billion** by late 2025, while China’s basic medical insurance system covered about **1.327 billion** people with participation above **95 percent**. National e-government also has scale: official commentary reported more than **1.08 billion** real-name users on the national integrated government-service platform by May 2025, with over 90 percent of provincial administrative licensing items available for online handling. citeturn35search0turn35search4turn35search1turn35search15

That matters because once AI tools are approved, China can plug them into already-centralized administrative channels. The 2025 State Council opinion on “AI+” explicitly calls for AI-enabled government service, public-safety management, rural service extension, and public-resource transaction supervision. Shanghai’s “One Net” governance reforms and Beijing’s unified application and data-platform efforts show how this administrative backbone is being localized. Beijing’s municipal data authority reported more than **2,011** items on its unified application platform in 2024–2025 and active work on government large-model applications; Shanghai continues to deepen its “一网通办” digital public-service architecture. citeturn8search10turn35search3turn22search10

Local implementation is crucial because China converts national strategy into outcomes through **provincial and municipal competition**. Beijing is building an application ecosystem around compute, data, model adaptation, and industrial use cases. Shanghai is pairing scenario openness with large-model incubation and compliance services. Shenzhen is pushing aggressively on AI terminals, embodied robots, and open application scenes. Hangzhou is using open source, “compute enclaves,” and a “compute supermarket” model. Jiangsu is leveraging manufacturing depth and setting very high penetration targets for terminals and agents. Guangdong combines provincial manufacturing heft with strong municipal hubs in Shenzhen and Guangzhou. This local-state activism is one of China’s strongest conversion mechanisms. citeturn40search0turn40search1turn34search6turn34search10turn10search11turn10search15turn23search2turn25search5turn11search4

Social acceptance is the final enabling condition. Survey evidence is imperfect, but it points in a consistent direction: Chinese respondents appear more optimistic and more willing to use AI than respondents in many advanced economies. Stanford’s 2025 AI Index reports that **83 percent** of respondents in China viewed AI products and services as more beneficial than harmful, compared with **39 percent** in the United States. A 2025 University of Melbourne–KPMG global study found China among the countries with the highest trust and acceptance levels, with over **60 percent** willing to trust AI and at least **49 percent** reporting high acceptance; KPMG’s China release said Chinese workers were especially heavy users of workplace AI. A parallel U.S.-China survey on AI governance found that respondents in both countries trust their own government most, but overall trust levels were higher among Chinese respondents. None of this proves durable legitimacy, but it does suggest a relatively permissive social environment for deployment. citeturn36search1turn37view2turn37view3turn36search0turn14search0

## Constraints, frictions, and the U.S. comparison

The biggest single limit on China’s AI conversion capacity remains **chips and frontier compute**. Brookings argues that Chinese labs, especially startups, remain constrained by advanced-chip export controls and limited capital. MERICS likewise concludes that hardware constraints still impede broader deployment even as Chinese models and applications advance rapidly. This means that China can sometimes compensate through efficiency, model compression, or open-source diffusion, but cannot yet assume unconstrained scaling at the frontier. citeturn16search9turn30search2turn16search3

The second constraint is **capital quality rather than capital quantity**. The state can marshal large funds—official sources say a **RMB 60 billion** national AI fund was launched in 2025—and local governments are creating layers of municipal funds, compute vouchers, and industrial subsidies. But external analyses from Carnegie, MERICS, and Rhodium warn that China’s broader industrial model creates strong risks of overinvestment, duplication, and overcapacity. In AI, that could mean too many parks, too many subsidized models, too many local platforms, and not enough commercially validated demand. citeturn31search4turn31search0turn31search1turn17search9turn29search3turn29search13

The third constraint is **labor absorption and social stability**. Chinese policy documents repeatedly acknowledge that AI can disrupt employment. The Fourteenth Five-Year employment plan called for tracking and coordinated response to AI’s employment effects, and the Fifteenth Five-Year Plan now calls for a formal mechanism to investigate and respond to AI’s labor-market impact, with job-retention support, retraining, and employment assistance. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security is now studying dedicated response measures. That is strong evidence that the leadership sees labor displacement as a real governance issue, not a hypothetical one. citeturn38search10turn38search2turn38search6turn38search9

That concern is sharpened by demographics and employment stress. China’s population fell again in 2025, by **3.39 million**, while the share of people aged 60 and above continues to rise. Overall urban surveyed unemployment averaged **5.2 percent** in 2025, and officials have repeatedly flagged pressure on youth and migrant-worker employment. AI and robotics may be economically attractive in an aging society, but they can still produce short- to medium-term instability if automation outpaces labor reallocation. citeturn29search1turn29search9turn29search12turn29search4

The fourth constraint is **openness and information quality**. China’s filing regime, algorithm rules, deep-synthesis labeling rules, and generative-AI content obligations are designed to preserve political control and social stability. That may help the state legitimize deployment, but it also likely narrows some categories of open experimentation, global collaboration, and data diversity. This is partly an inference from the design of the rules themselves, but it is a reasonable one: systems optimized for political safety and traceability will not always be optimized for the widest possible creativity or information openness. citeturn43search0turn43search1turn43search2turn19search10

Compared with the United States, then, China appears to have a **deployment advantage in concentrated, policy-steered domains** and a **frontier disadvantage in top-end capability inputs**. The United States has the stronger frontier ecosystem; China may have the stronger state-led **diffusion machine**. The most plausible outcome is not a single winner, but a divergence: America remains stronger at frontier research and general-purpose AI platforms, while China becomes especially strong at **industrial AI, embodied AI, smart terminals, and AI-infused public administration**. citeturn14search10turn16search9turn17search14turn30search16

In net assessment, China appears more likely than the United States to achieve **rapid, state-steered AI conversion in manufacturing, robotics, terminals, and administratively governed public services**. It appears less likely than the United States to dominate the frontier of general-purpose AI on chips, compute, and top-model production. The decisive strategic question for the atlas is therefore not “Who has better AI?” but **“Who can operationalize sufficiently strong AI inside the larger economic system?”** On that narrower but very consequential question, the evidence suggests China may have a real advantage in several major domains—provided its chip bottlenecks, labor frictions, and investment distortions do not intensify faster than its deployment gains. citeturn16search9turn14search10turn41search1turn35search15turn29search13turn38search2
