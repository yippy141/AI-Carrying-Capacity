# AI Conversion Atlas Comparator Report

## Comparative framing and matrix

This report benchmarks the United States, the EU/Germany, Japan, South Korea, Singapore, and India as comparators for China on ten dimensions that matter for “AI conversion”: not only frontier-model capability, but also the harder problem of converting AI into usable productive capacity across energy systems, factories, firms, government, and labor markets. The evidence base is deliberately weighted toward official strategies, laws, public statistics, public procurement guidance, think tank and academic work, and industry or infrastructure datasets. Searches were conducted in English and relevant local languages, including German, French, Japanese, and Korean official sources. citeturn40search1turn34view4turn34view3turn35view5turn35view2turn34view5

The core analytic distinction is between **frontier capability** and **conversion capability**. A country can have strong access to frontier models and still struggle to translate that into productivity if it lacks power, interconnection, industrial software integration, procurement pathways, training capacity, or politically legitimate adaptation mechanisms. Conversely, a country can lag in domestic frontier models yet still convert AI effectively if it has strong robotics, factory integration, state demand, and worker-adjustment institutions. That distinction is especially important when comparing these systems with China, where frontier-model performance, industrial automation, and infrastructure rollout do not move in lockstep. citeturn33search1turn33search9turn30search10turn33search2

The matrix below uses qualitative ratings. “Very strong” means the country or region has both visible policy commitment and already-deployed capacity at meaningful scale. “Strong” means the architecture exists and is expanding, but one or two conversion constraints remain binding. “Moderate” means the institutional architecture exists but diffusion is incomplete or uneven. “Limited” means capability remains narrow, pilot-heavy, or structurally constrained.

These ratings synthesize the evidence documented in the country briefs below. The broad pattern is that the United States remains the strongest overall frontier ecosystem; Germany and Japan remain two of the most credible “industrial conversion” comparators; South Korea is the most explicitly state-directed manufacturing-to-AI converter outside China; Singapore is the strongest public-sector and governance converter per unit of national size; India is the largest “option value” case, where digital public infrastructure and scale create upside, but where conversion is still far behind headline ambition. citeturn33search1turn37search0turn40search4turn34view3turn14search4turn20search4turn34view5

## United States

The United States is still the strongest frontier-AI ecosystem in this comparison set. Stanford’s 2025 and 2026 AI Index reporting shows that U.S.-based institutions continue to produce more notable or top-tier models than China or Europe, even though the U.S.-China frontier performance gap has narrowed sharply and Chinese models have become near peers on several major benchmarks. That means the U.S. advantage is no longer simply “best model wins”; it is increasingly an ecosystem advantage composed of leading labs, cloud platforms, capital markets, research universities, and a broad commercial vendor stack. On standards and safety governance, NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework remains the most internationally influential voluntary framework, and NIST is extending it into sector-specific work such as trustworthy AI in critical infrastructure. citeturn33search1turn33search9turn37search8turn40search6turn40search10

Where the U.S. looks less dominant is the physical layer. The White House’s 2025 *America’s AI Action Plan* explicitly treats permitting, data centers, semiconductors, and power generation as the core infrastructure problem of the AI era. The plan calls for streamlined permitting for data centers and energy infrastructure, for AI-assisted environmental review, and for a grid strategy built around preserving dispatchable generation, upgrading transmission, and accelerating interconnection. That framing is not rhetorical. DOE’s 2024 data-center electricity report found that data centers consumed about 4.4% of U.S. electricity in 2023 and could reach roughly 6.7% to 12% by 2028, while FERC has spent 2025 and 2026 forcing grid operators to clarify or reform large-load and co-location rules for AI data centers. In other words, the U.S. can finance and design frontier AI faster than it can always power and permit it. citeturn41view0turn41view1turn41view2turn38search1turn38search2turn38search5turn38search11

The industrial-conversion picture is better than the infrastructure headlines sometimes suggest, but it is uneven. The U.S. manufacturing base is less robot-dense than Korea, Singapore, Germany, Japan, or China, yet it is not static. IFR’s preliminary 2026 release reports U.S. industrial robot installations rose 11% year on year to 38,000 units in 2025, with recovery broadening beyond automotive. Manufacturing USA’s ARM Institute gives the U.S. a real, if decentralized, robotics-and-AI conversion channel: its mission is explicitly to deploy robotics by combining manufacturing practice, sensor systems, software, AI, and workforce training across a consortium that now spans hundreds of organizations. The U.S. model is therefore not one national factory-upgrade plan, but a patchwork of regional manufacturing institutes, vendor-led diffusion, large-system integrators, and hyperscaler partnerships. That can be innovative, but also uneven by state, sector, and firm size. citeturn30search14turn40search2turn40search5turn40search9turn40search13

On business adoption, the U.S. is further along than most comparators in measured enterprise use. Census Bureau BTOS data for the period from December 2025 to May 2026 show overall AI usage hovering around 17% to 20% of businesses, with 20% to 23% expecting to use AI within six months, and substantially higher rates in larger firms. That matters because the U.S. is one of the few countries now producing near-real-time official survey data on AI use by sector, geography, and firm size. The deeper issue is that adoption is still concentrated: frontier access is ubiquitous in theory, but organizational conversion is uneven in practice. The relevant U.S. bottleneck is no longer only “can firms access a model?” but “can they redesign workflows, data governance, cybersecurity, and management systems around it?” citeturn37search0turn37search2turn37search3turn37search19turn37search11

The U.S. state is also moving faster than in 2023–2024 to become a meaningful AI customer and coordinator. OMB Memorandum M-25-21 governs federal agency AI use and requires innovation with safeguards rather than blanket hesitation. OMB M-25-22 does the same for acquisition, giving agencies new procurement guidance to buy AI “quickly, competitively, and responsibly.” The White House fact sheet presenting those memos explicitly frames them as barriers-removal measures. GSA has built an operational procurement layer on top of this policy foundation, including dedicated “Buy AI” pathways, use-case inventories, community-of-practice resources, and agency compliance plans. This is important strategically: public procurement is not just about government productivity, but also about whether the state can create a domestic market for trustworthy enterprise AI. citeturn34view1turn39search2turn39search8turn39search10turn39search14turn39search16turn39search3turn39search5turn39search11turn39search19turn39search15

On workforce and labor adaptation, the U.S. is institutionally more attentive than many critics assume, but still far from having a comprehensive adjustment regime. The Action Plan calls for recurring analysis and scenario planning on AI’s labor effects, rapid response funds for worker displacement, pilots using existing workforce authorities, and a national initiative to identify the occupations needed for AI infrastructure buildout. BLS, meanwhile, has begun formally incorporating AI into employment-projections methods and continues to publish occupational outlooks that point to growth in software, data, and IT infrastructure roles even as routine clerical and some retail roles face pressure. The problem is not lack of awareness; it is that adjustment in the U.S. still depends heavily on state programs, employer initiative, and local labor-market institutions rather than a unified national compact. citeturn41view3turn37search1turn37search4turn37search7turn37search9

Governance and legitimacy are therefore mixed. On the one hand, the U.S. has the deepest open standard-setting ecosystem in this field: NIST, OMB, GSA, and sector agencies generate unusually transparent guidance, inventories, and procurement rules. On the other hand, the state remains fragmented across federal agencies, states, utilities, and regional grid operators. The White House’s own energy and permitting language is revealing: it treats interconnection, dispatchable capacity, and environmental review as central obstacles to AI dominance. A system that is highly innovative at the frontier remains structurally slower at building the infrastructure and labor institutions that determine whether AI becomes broad-based productivity rather than enclave productivity. citeturn41view0turn41view2turn38search7turn39search10turn40search6

Security relevance is where the U.S. still has the greatest state capacity. DoD’s 2023 AI adoption strategy and subsequent Rapid Capabilities Cell work are aimed at scaling generative and advanced AI across warfighting and enterprise uses. The 2026 defense AI strategy ties AI adoption directly to “war-winning efforts,” while a June 2026 National Security Presidential Memorandum directs the national security enterprise to adapt commercial and open-source AI from diverse suppliers. The U.S. therefore retains a much stronger openly documented civil-military AI integration pathway than most comparators. The binding constraints are not vision or demand. They are electricity, permitting, skilled-trades scarcity for physical buildout, and the still-incomplete diffusion of AI from elite firms and agencies into the long tail of American business. citeturn26search3turn26search9turn26search12turn26search15turn26search18turn26search0turn39search18

## EU and Germany

The EU/Germany comparator is best understood as a hybrid system. At the EU level, the bloc is strongest in rule-making, market-shaping, and shared digital/public infrastructures. At the Germany level, the strength lies in industrial process know-how, advanced manufacturing, sectoral depth, and the institutional capacity to organize diffusion through standards, industrial associations, and cooperative vocational structures. France matters inside this comparator because it is the clearest continental attempt to build an indigenous frontier-AI layer through France 2030, AI clusters, the Jean Zay supercomputer, and a national strategy that explicitly links AI to sovereignty. The result is a European/continental model that is less frontier-dominant than the United States, but better organized around trustworthy deployment and industrial embedding. citeturn36search1turn36search3turn36search6turn34view4turn28search2turn28search5turn28search17

On governance, the AI Act is the defining fact. It entered into force on 1 August 2024 and becomes fully applicable on 2 August 2026, with staggered earlier obligations for some categories, including GPAI rules that began applying from 2 August 2025. The AI Act’s importance for conversion is often misunderstood. It is not only a compliance instrument; it is a market-structuring instrument that tries to create common trust, documentation, and conformity assumptions across the single market. The AI Pact and AI Office are part of the same logic: reduce uncertainty, socialize compliance, and help firms, including SMEs, learn the new rules before full applicability. That can slow some experimentation, but it can also lower transaction costs for adoption in regulated sectors once the rules are internalized. citeturn36search0turn36search1turn36search3turn36search6turn36search9turn36search12

Frontier AI access is where Europe remains structurally weaker than the United States. The bloc is not absent: France’s strategy seeks to place the country among European and global AI leaders; the first phase of the national French strategy financed interdisciplinary institutes, excellence chairs, doctoral programs, and Jean Zay, and the second phase under France 2030 adds another €1 billion focused on diffusion, talent, deep tech supply, and generative AI. France 2030 overall dedicates roughly €2.5 billion to AI, and the nine selected AI clusters receive large multi-year support. But even with that effort, continental frontier access still depends heavily on U.S. cloud capacity and a relatively small number of domestic champions. Europe is building a serious layer, but not yet a U.S.-style frontier ecosystem. citeturn34view4turn28search2turn28search5turn28search8turn28search14

Germany’s biggest recent move is on compute and energy infrastructure. In March 2026, the federal government adopted a national data-center strategy that aims to double data-center IT connection capacity by 2030 relative to 2025 and to at least quadruple connection capacity for HPC and AI. The strategy is explicit that high-performance compute for AI and cloud is a sovereignty and competitiveness issue. At the same time, Germany’s 2023 Energy Efficiency Act imposes energy-efficiency and waste-heat requirements on data centers, and the BMWK’s data-center registry and sector reports are trying to make the physical and energy profile of the sector more transparent. This dual approach is distinctive: Germany is trying to build more compute, but it is also trying to discipline that buildout through efficiency and heat-use obligations. citeturn35view3turn29search1turn29search4turn29search11turn29search8turn29search17turn29search0

That produces both strength and vulnerability. The strength is legal and infrastructure visibility: Germany knows it needs more AI-appropriate compute and is creating administrative and statistical infrastructure around data centers. The vulnerability is cost and speed. German and wider European electricity remains more expensive than in major U.S. hubs, and the siting, grid, and climate-policy overlay raises the cost of rapid hyperscale rollouts. Germany’s own official rhetoric increasingly frames compute as a sovereignty problem precisely because it recognizes that it is competing against U.S. and Chinese systems operating at larger energy and capital scale. citeturn35view3turn29search10turn29search0

Industrial conversion is where this comparator becomes much stronger. Germany is still one of the world’s most automated factory economies. IFR’s 2025 industrial-robot summary places Germany at 449 robots per 10,000 manufacturing employees, fourth globally, and one of the five largest robot markets by stock and annual installations. More importantly, Germany pairs automation with strong industrial process engineering, supplier networks, and software-intensive manufacturing traditions. On the enterprise side, Destatis reported that 26% of German firms with at least 10 employees used AI in 2025, including 57% of large firms, 36% of medium firms, and 23% of firms with 10–49 employees. At the EU level, Eurostat reported 19.95% of EU enterprises used AI in 2025, while 55.03% of large enterprises did. This is not mass diffusion yet, but it is substantially higher than Europe’s 2023–2024 baseline and shows that firm-level adoption is becoming measurable and real. citeturn30search10turn30search12turn40search1turn40search4turn40search0turn40search3

SME diffusion still needs help, and France is the clearest example of targeted policy. IA Booster France 2030 offers subsidized support for SMEs and mid-caps, with a €25 million budget, and official reporting says it had already sensitized more than 1,000 firms and supported more than 300 in identifying relevant AI use cases. France’s new “Osez l’IA” plan pushes in the same direction, using ambassadors, business networks, and matching mechanisms to move from elite research to broad enterprise use. Germany has many equivalent diffusion institutions, but the national picture is more decentralized and less tied to one flagship AI-branding campaign. That decentralization fits its economic structure, but it also makes AI conversion less legible than in Singapore or Korea. citeturn28search0turn28search1turn28search13turn28search4turn28search9turn28search10

Workforce and legitimacy are relative strengths. France’s strategy sets explicit talent goals, including training 40,000 to 100,000 students per year in AI-related areas and funding additional theses, while Germany’s broader industrial and vocational system remains a strong platform for retraining and shop-floor adaptation. The EU model also has stronger worker-information, consultation, and social-dialogue traditions than the United States, which may help with legitimacy as AI moves deeper into regulated workplaces. Still, real bottlenecks remain. The region lacks U.S.-style venture depth, faces higher energy costs, and operates through a fragmented single market in which compliance can become a burden before diffusional benefits appear. Europe and Germany can plausibly become very high-quality AI converters in industry and regulated sectors. Their challenge is doing so at continental scale and speed. citeturn34view4turn28search5turn29search16turn36search11

## Japan

Japan’s AI trajectory is defined by an unusual combination of deep industrial capability and official self-critique. The Cabinet-approved *Artificial Intelligence Basic Plan* of December 2025 is striking because it says plainly that, despite rapid global diffusion of generative AI, AI has not yet become something actively used in daily life and work in Japan, and that Japan has been falling behind not only major powers but even smaller economies in AI-related development and investment. The document also makes clear that AI is now understood as directly relevant to industrial competitiveness and national security. That frank self-assessment is analytically important: Japan’s problem is not that it lacks advanced firms or robots. It is that diffusion, platform scaling, and commercial AI investment have lagged the country’s industrial potential. citeturn34view3

Frontier access in Japan is therefore best described as strategically important but still thinner than in the U.S. or China. METI’s semiconductor and digital industry strategy explicitly ties together advanced semiconductors, data centers, and the social implementation of generative AI. Japan has also institutionalized an AI safety layer through the Japan AI Safety Institute, which frames safety not as a brake on innovation but as part of a strategy to promote standards, evaluation methods, and international cooperation. In policy terms, Japan is trying to avoid being forced into a choice between frontier participation and safe deployment. Yet the government’s own documents suggest that indigenous frontier scale remains limited compared with the largest U.S. and Chinese ecosystems. Japan’s position is strongest where AI intersects with robotics, manufacturing, and high-quality sectoral data, not where it depends on massive foundation-model capital expenditure alone. citeturn10search0turn11search0turn11search11turn11search3

Compute and energy infrastructure are a central constraint. Japan’s digital-infrastructure debate has long emphasized concentration around Tokyo and Osaka, and a METI digital infrastructure council presentation highlighted that roughly 85% of data centers and 98% of internet exchanges were concentrated there, with major power-demand implications. The same material framed data-center electricity use as a geographical and energy-system problem, not merely a digital one. By 2026, Japan’s energy authorities were openly asking how to manage rising electricity demand from data centers, and the broader industrial strategy placed generative AI, semiconductors, and data centers into one policy bundle. This means Japan sees compute not as an isolated tech-sector issue but as a regional planning and power-systems issue. That is a strength in strategic clarity, but it also reveals a hard constraint: Japan has less room than the U.S. or China for unconstrained hyperscale buildout. citeturn10search1turn10search5turn10search0

Industrial conversion, by contrast, is a Japanese strength. IFR’s 2025 summary places Japan at 446 robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers, among the most automated economies in the world, and Japan remains one of the top global robot markets by both installations and stock. METI’s 2026 robotics policy direction explicitly foregrounds AI robotics and social implementation. The country also continues to use the broader Society 5.0 frame in infrastructure-heavy areas such as construction, where *i-Construction 2.0* pushes automation, remote inspection, 3D models, and large-scale data use. Japan therefore has one of the strongest real-economy bases in this comparison set for moving from digital AI to embodied AI. If “AI conversion” increasingly means AI plus machines plus scarce labor plus physical work, Japan is structurally well placed. citeturn30search10turn30search12turn8search10turn10search9

The weakness is enterprise and organizational diffusion. IPA’s *DX Trends 2025* survey, comparing Japan, the United States, and Germany, found Japanese firms were relatively strong on cost reduction and efficiency gains from digital transformation, but weaker than U.S. and German firms on revenue growth, profit growth, and customer-satisfaction outcomes. That fits a common pattern in Japan: technology adoption is frequently justified first as efficiency improvement rather than as aggressive business-model reinvention. METI’s 2025 survey on generative AI similarly describes a rapidly expanding but still early-stage market structure. The implication for the Atlas is that Japan may convert AI into operational excellence faster than into breakout new business models. That can still matter enormously in manufacturing, logistics, and public services, but it produces a different growth profile than the U.S. model. citeturn9search1turn9search3

The state is trying to speed up public-sector adoption more directly. In May 2025, the Digital Agency issued a government guideline on the procurement and use of generative AI for administrative evolution and innovation, explicitly coupling promotion of use with risk management. This is a significant step because Japan has often relied on soft-law and administrative guidance to socialize new technology across ministries and contractors. Rather than waiting for a single overarching AI statute to solve implementation, Tokyo is using procurement guidance, ministry coordination, and sectoral rules to create usable pathways into government work. That is a pragmatic fit for the Japanese state. It is also a reminder that Japan’s governance model is still more coordination-heavy and principle-based than the EU’s hard-regulation approach. citeturn35view5turn11search0

Workforce and labor are a central motive, not just a side issue. Japan’s AI planning repeatedly links AI adoption to labor shortages, demographic aging, and the need to maintain service and production capacity. IPA’s 2025 discussion of “digital talent in the AI era” emphasizes continuing deficits in both the quantity and quality of digital talent inside firms. This is where Japan’s AI strategy is deeply conversion-oriented: AI is supposed to offset labor scarcity in industry, administration, and society, not simply create a new startup sector. That creates legitimacy for adoption, but it also raises expectations. If AI diffusion remains slow, the country’s demographic constraints become even more binding. citeturn34view3turn9search4turn11search8

Security relevance is high and increasingly explicit. The defense white paper for 2025, the FY2026 budget materials, and the new next-generation information and communication strategy all place digital and AI-relevant capabilities inside the broader Japanese defense modernization effort. Japan’s AI framing is therefore dual-use in a very literal sense: industrial competitiveness, administrative modernization, and national defense are now discussed as part of a shared capability problem. The bottlenecks are clear. Japan still lacks the frontier investment depth of the United States, faces geographic and power concentration in its data-center system, and must overcome slow organizational change in risk-averse enterprises. But among advanced industrial economies, few have a better embodied-AI runway. citeturn26search1turn26search4turn26search7turn26search10

## South Korea

South Korea is the comparator that most directly resembles China in policy style, though not in scale. It combines a strong manufacturing base, a world-leading robotics profile, a strategic semiconductor sector, and increasingly explicit whole-of-government AI planning. Korea’s AI Basic Act was passed in December 2024, and by 2026 the National AI Strategy Committee had elevated AI into a 12-domain action architecture covering infrastructure, public-sector transformation, regional transformation, culture, and even “AI-powered defense leadership.” Korea’s official direction is not simply “encourage AI.” It is “organize a national AI conversion program with sectoral, regional, and public-administration tracks.” That is a very different posture from the more decentralized U.S. and German models. citeturn12search0turn12search3turn13search0turn31search5

The compute story is central to this strategy. In 2024, MSIT announced policy directions that explicitly recognized Korea’s shortage of AI computing infrastructure relative to global big tech. The plan aims to increase GPU capability fifteen-fold to more than two exaflops by 2030, establish a National AI Computing Center valued at up to KRW 2 trillion via public-private partnership, and support private AI-computing infrastructure through low-interest loans through 2027. Korea’s English-language and Korean releases both stress that the National AI Computing Center is not just a datacenter project; it is meant to catalyze an ecosystem spanning AI semiconductors, cloud, and AI-enabled services. This is a clear example of conversion-oriented policy: compute is treated as an industrial commons, not just a market good. citeturn31search7turn34view6turn31search3turn35view6

Industrial conversion is Korea’s clearest structural advantage. IFR’s 2025 data place Korea first in the world on robot density, at 1,220 robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers, far above every other comparator. The manufacturing strategy is being updated to exploit that base. According to Korea’s 2026 industrial policy releases, spending related to AI factory pilot projects rose from KRW 158.2 billion in 2025 to KRW 220 billion in 2026, with an official target of building more than 500 autonomous-manufacturing AI factories by 2030; policy communications around the M.AX alliance also described a path from roughly 102 AI factories in 2025 to 500 by 2030. Korea is thus attempting to move from “robotized manufacturing” to “AI-orchestrated manufacturing” with explicit numeric targets, sector-specific projects, and alliance structures. citeturn30search10turn14search4turn14search6

Where Korea is less advanced is broad SME diffusion. That weakness is visible enough that the Ministry of SMEs and Startups made it a headline policy target in February 2025, stating that SME AI utilization was 28% in 2024 and setting a goal of 50%, alongside a goal of nurturing five global AI unicorns. The same ministry later launched a region-led AI transformation program with a KRW 35 billion budget in supplementary funding, designed to let local governments tailor AI diffusion to local industrial structure. This matters because Korea’s conversion challenge is not whether Samsung, SK, Hyundai, or top labs can use AI. It is whether the supplier ecosystem beyond the chaebol can adopt it in time to matter for national productivity. citeturn14search5turn14search1

The Korean state is also moving rapidly on procurement and internal adoption. In June 2026, MOIS formally announced the “AI Democratic Government” strategy as the organizing framework for public-sector AI transformation, presenting it as a whole-of-government AX roadmap rather than a short-term technology plan. Earlier in 2026, MOIS launched a KRW 18 billion public AI service support program to accelerate adoption across central and local government, and then published a *Guideline for the Introduction and Utilization of AI in the Public Sector* built around a common government AI platform. Korea’s finance ministry, meanwhile, said public procurement would be used strategically to help scale AI and robot firms and create an “AI procurement ecosystem.” This is one of the strongest public-adoption and public-demand signals in the comparator set. citeturn18search1turn18search2turn18search3turn35view2turn18search12turn35view4turn16search2

Workforce strategy is similarly state-led. Korea’s education policy introduced AI digital textbooks beginning in 2025 and aims to scale the system through 2028; the broader action-plan ecosystem also emphasizes core AI talent. This is not a complete labor-adjustment regime, but it does mean Korea is trying to normalize AI use through school systems, public administration, and manufacturing simultaneously. That increases the likelihood of broad societal familiarity with AI tools compared with countries that leave learning primarily to firms. It also creates political ownership: AI becomes a national-development project rather than merely a private technology wave. citeturn13search1turn13search13turn13search0

Governance is moving from policy declaration to legal infrastructure. The AI Basic Act gives Korea a more explicit statutory base than Japan’s historically softer approach, while public-sector guides and national strategies show the state trying to standardize implementation. At the same time, Korea’s own policy texts keep returning to the same binding bottleneck: compute. The government’s repeated references to shortages in AI infrastructure, the need for domestic AI chips, and financing support for private buildout show that Korea’s strategic ambition is ahead of its current hardware base. This is the central Korean constraint. The country has world-class chips, world-class manufacturing firms, world-leading robot density, and a highly capable state. But it still lacks the scale of domestic frontier compute and model ecosystems enjoyed by the largest powers. citeturn34view6turn31search7turn12search3

Security relevance is also unusually explicit in Korean strategy. The National AI Strategy Committee’s 12-domain framework includes “AI-powered defense leadership,” and Korea’s broader state vocabulary links AI, semiconductors, networks, and public infrastructure as strategic industries. Even where operational detail is not public, the public documents make clear that Korea sees AI not only as an economic issue but as a national-security capability stack. In the AI Conversion Atlas, South Korea should therefore be treated as perhaps the strongest medium-sized “full-stack converter” in the dataset: not because it matches U.S. frontier scale, but because it integrates compute, manufacturing, public adoption, and national strategy more tightly than almost anyone else. citeturn31search5turn31search9

## Singapore

Singapore is the most coherent state-led converter in this comparison, even if it is physically too small to compete on absolute scale. The country’s National AI Strategy 2.0, and the May 2026 update to that strategy, frame AI as “AI for the Public Good, for Singapore and the World.” That slogan is not empty branding. It captures the core Singaporean model: rather than trying to outspend the United States or outbuild China, Singapore is organizing AI around governance credibility, rapid public-service use, targeted enterprise diffusion, and carefully managed digital infrastructure. In a report intended to benchmark conversion rather than frontier glamour, that makes Singapore disproportionately important. citeturn34view7turn19search7

Frontier access is stronger than Singapore’s domestic model base might suggest. Because Singapore is a highly open, trade- and services-oriented economy, firms and agencies can access frontier AI primarily through global cloud and software ecosystems rather than through indigenous foundation models. The policy emphasis has therefore gone into trust and assurance. IMDA and the AI Verify Foundation have turned Singapore into one of the most visible testing grounds for responsible AI governance, launching the Global AI Assurance Pilot in 2025, publishing a model AI governance framework for generative AI, releasing a starter kit for testing LLM-based applications, and, in 2026, publishing a model framework for agentic AI. This is a genuine comparative advantage. Singapore cannot dominate model training at global scale, but it can become a preferred jurisdiction for governance-ready deployment. citeturn19search0turn19search3turn19search9turn19search12turn19search15turn19search18

Compute and energy are where the city-state’s physical constraints show most clearly. The government’s Green Data Centre Roadmap, refreshed Green Mark standards for data centers, and data-center energy-efficiency grants are all designed around the same reality: Singapore wants more AI compute, but only under strict land, power, and sustainability constraints. EDB said in 2024 that Singapore planned to expand data-center capacity by at least one-third, adding at least 300 MW in the next few years and another 200 MW for operators using green-energy options. At the same time, the energy mix remains overwhelmingly gas-based: EMA data show natural gas provided 94.0% of electricity generation in 2024. Singapore’s grid is exceptionally reliable, but its AI-compute future is constrained by land, imported energy dependence, and the need to prove “tropical” data-center sustainability. citeturn21search14turn21search0turn21search8turn21search12turn21search16turn21search1turn21search13

Industrial conversion is better than many outsiders assume, though still bounded by economic structure. Singapore has one of the world’s highest robot densities: IFR’s 2025 data place it second only to Korea, at 818 robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers. That reflects its role in high-value electronics, semiconductors, precision engineering, and process industries rather than a giant labor-intensive factory base. The more telling signal is firm-level adoption. IMDA’s 2024 and 2025 digital-economy reporting shows AI adoption among non-SMEs rising sharply, from 44% in 2023 to 62.5% in 2024, while SME adoption rose from 4.2% to 14.5% over the same period. Those are still uneven numbers, but they show a real shift from exploratory to operational use. citeturn30search10turn35view1turn20search4turn20search5

Enterprise and SME diffusion are supported by a relatively sophisticated state toolkit. The Digital Economy Report series links adoption not only to technology uptake but to business outcomes. For example, IMDA has reported that SMEs adopting digital solutions under the Productivity Solutions Grant saw average cost savings of around 50% over 2018–2023. Sector-specific playbooks then build on that base. The government and related agencies have published generative-AI playbooks for enterprises and for particular sectors such as tourism, trying to translate AI from abstract excitement into applied use cases. Singapore’s policy instinct is relentlessly practical: identify a use case, create a playbook, attach grants or advisory support, and then measure outcomes. That tends to produce less hype and more institutional memory than in larger countries. citeturn35view1turn19search1turn19search4turn20search10

The public sector is where Singapore is most distinctive. GovTech’s AIBots initiative gives officers in government agencies usable internal AI tools, and one official government case study claims that a task which had previously cost roughly S$20,000 and several weeks of consultant and officer time could be reduced to two days of work by one officer using AIBots. The Public Service leadership has also moved toward mandatory AI literacy, with the Public Service Division saying in 2025 that all officers would take a mandatory AI literacy course. This is conversion in the strongest sense: not just buying AI, but making the bureaucracy itself more AI-capable. Few countries have matched Singapore’s visible speed in mainstreaming internal state use. citeturn22search1turn22search4turn22search6

Workforce adaptation is likewise broader than elite STEM training. IMDA’s 2024–2025 annual reporting says Singapore expanded its Digital Skills for Life framework to include generative AI skills and had already reached more than 370,000 seniors through Digital Ambassadors. That is relevant because social legitimacy in AI policy often depends less on national AI champions than on whether ordinary citizens feel they can still navigate work, services, and daily life. Singapore’s inclusion strategy is unusually explicit and unusually administrative. This helps explain why the government can move quickly on public AI use without triggering the same degree of distrust seen elsewhere. citeturn20search7turn19search16

Security relevance is publicly visible but narrower in scale than that of the United States or Japan. MINDEF has said Singapore has had guiding principles for responsible, robust, reliable, and safe AI in defense since 2021; the Digital and Intelligence Service is incorporating AI into critical-infrastructure defense and cyber talent programs; and public speeches frame AI as part of the future-security toolkit. The result is a city-state model of AI conversion that is unusually coherent across governance, public services, digital trust, and targeted industrial use, but still constrained by physical scale, land, and power. Singapore’s lesson for the Atlas is that state capacity can compensate for size, but not fully overcome energy geography. citeturn26search11turn26search2turn26search20turn26search8

## India

India is the largest upside case in this comparison set, and also the one with the widest gap between infrastructural promise and actual conversion. The Cabinet-approved IndiaAI Mission, launched in March 2024 with a budget outlay of ₹10,371.92 crore over five years, is ambitious and unusually explicit. It aims to build a full ecosystem: public AI compute, indigenous foundation models, startup financing, safe and trusted AI tools, and broad-based access to data and skills. The mission’s framing, “Making AI in India and Making AI Work for India,” is analytically important. India’s official strategy is not mainly about exporting frontier prestige. It is about democratizing access to AI across a vast and unequal economy. citeturn34view5turn23search5turn23search9

Compute is the first test of whether that aspiration turns into reality. The original mission promised 10,000 or more GPUs through a public-private model. By early 2026, official PIB guidance on India’s AI governance framework said more than 38,000 GPUs had been onboarded under the IndiaAI Mission, with a target of 100,000 and subsidized access via the IndiaAI Compute Portal; it also referenced AIKosh, more than 40 petaflops of supercomputing under the National Supercomputing Mission, and systems such as AIRAWAT and PARAM Siddhi-AI. IndiaAI media materials further stated that an additional 20,000 GPUs would be added. These are meaningful numbers for a country starting from low shared compute availability, but they should still be read as ecosystem-building rather than proof of U.S.- or China-scale frontier capacity. India is improving access, not yet matching the largest global AI infrastructure clusters. citeturn24search5turn23search4turn23search0

What India has that no other comparator quite matches is digital public infrastructure at population scale. Official government materials describe India’s DPI stack as serving more than 1.4 billion people at very low cost, underpinned by digital identity, payments, public platforms, and wide regulatory support. Multiple government and policy documents now explicitly describe AI and DPI as complementary: DPI provides the rails for population-scale deployment, while AI can add automation, personalization, language access, and decision support. MeitY and related presentations have also discussed creating AI curation units in government ministries, and the Ministry of Statistics’ Data Innovation Lab has published official casebooks on AI use in public statistics. This makes India one of the few countries where public-sector AI adoption could plausibly scale quickly once usable models, datasets, and procurement pathways are available. citeturn24search9turn24search7turn23search7turn23search3turn23search11

Industrial and manufacturing conversion is more mixed. India clearly wants AI-enabled advanced manufacturing. NITI Aayog’s 2025 roadmap for advanced manufacturing and MeitY’s draft National Strategy on Robotics both position AI, robotics, and cyber-physical systems as strategic enablers for industrial upgrading. Invest India and IndiaAI case materials document use cases in smart manufacturing and industrial automation. But the official and quasi-official evidence also shows that broad-based factory conversion is still uneven. India’s manufacturing base is large, but a great deal of it remains far from the kind of sensorized, software-rich production environment in which AI can scale easily. The Atlas should therefore treat India less as a current industrial AI leader than as a country with high latent upside if compute, management capability, and industrial digitization converge. citeturn25search1turn24search8turn25search2turn25search3turn25search6

The enterprise-adoption picture is similarly heterogeneous. India has a large AI startup ecosystem and strong software-services capacity, but the economy-wide diffusion of AI is harder to measure than in the United States, Germany, or Singapore. Official measurement is improving: MeitY’s 2025 work on estimating India’s digital economy found that the digital economy accounted for 14.67 million workers, or 2.55% of the workforce, in 2022–23. That is large in absolute numbers but still relatively limited relative to India’s total labor force. India’s AI conversion problem is thus not simply one of supplying talent; it is also a problem of managerial absorption, data readiness, power reliability, sectoral heterogeneity, and state capability across regions. The firms most ready to use AI are often already fairly digital; the long tail is much harder. citeturn24search4turn23search21

Workforce policy is broad and politically salient. Official statements tied to the IndiaAI Mission emphasize skilling, upskilling, and reskilling across the lifecycle, while NITI Aayog’s 2025 roadmap on job creation in the AI economy explicitly highlights gaps in computer science education, patents, and AI depth relative to leading countries. That combination matters. India has one of the deepest raw labor pools in the comparison, but also one of the greatest risks that workforce preparation, formal-sector job creation, and AI-driven automation will not move in sync. If the United States worries about regional displacement, India has to worry about scale, informality, and the quality of pathways from education to AI-enabled work. citeturn23search19turn25search11

Governance is still intentionally flexible. India’s January and February 2026 official AI governance releases describe a “techno-legal” framework rather than an EU-style hard-law code, seeking to preserve innovation while layering baseline legal protections, sectoral rules, technical controls, and institutional mechanisms. This may suit India’s developmental priorities better than a heavy ex ante regime. It also fits the DPI philosophy: set usable public rails, allow private and public experimentation, and manage risk through layered governance rather than through one comprehensive statute. The advantage is adaptability. The downside is uncertainty, especially for firms looking for predictable compliance expectations across sectors and states. citeturn24search17turn24search5

Security relevance is publicly present but much less detailed than in the U.S. or Japan in the sources reviewed. Indian official AI materials emphasize sovereignty, strategic technologies, semiconductors, and digital public infrastructure more often than detailed military AI doctrine. That in itself is revealing. India’s AI project is still overwhelmingly framed as developmental state-building. The binding bottlenecks are knowable: affordable compute, high-quality and interoperable datasets, stronger enterprise digitization beyond tech-forward firms, more reliable energy and infrastructure, and deeper labor-market pathways. If those improve, India could become a uniquely powerful AI converter because DPI gives it a distribution channel that others lack. If they do not, India risks becoming an AI user and services market without fully converting AI into broad productivity growth. citeturn24search15turn24search6turn23search21

## Indicators, data sources, bilateral takeaways, and measurement gaps

### Indicator candidates

The most useful indicator set for the AI Conversion Atlas should distinguish **access**, **conversion**, and **adaptation**. One practical approach is to track a small number of indicators under each heading and keep them as stable as possible across countries. For frontier access, the most defensible candidates are the number of notable frontier models based in-country or credibly available in-country; subsidized or public compute capacity; cloud-region presence; and availability of domestic or allied model suppliers. For compute and energy, use installed or contracted data-center IT capacity, grid interconnection lead times where available, electricity prices for industrial users, data-center electricity share, and whether there is a published national strategy linking AI to power and permitting. For industrial conversion, robot density, industrial robot installations, manufacturing software adoption, and AI use in manufacturing are the leading candidates. For enterprise diffusion, official enterprise-survey AI use by firm size is the cleanest comparable metric. For the state, track whether there is an AI procurement memorandum, a public-sector AI use inventory, common platforms, and measurable agency deployment. For labor adaptation, include AI-related workforce programs, occupational projections, AI literacy efforts, and where possible indicators of worker displacement support. citeturn33search1turn38search1turn40search3turn30search10turn37search0turn39search10turn23search21

A second principle is to prefer **official, regularly updated time series** over one-off survey numbers, even when the one-off number is more exciting. The U.S. Census BTOS and Eurostat enterprise AI tables are especially valuable because they are recurring instruments and can be broken out by firm size and sector. Singapore’s Digital Economy Report is useful because it combines adoption, workforce, and GDP framing in one recurring national publication. India’s official stack is improving, but still weaker on recurring firm-level AI-adoption measurement. Japan and Korea fall in between: both have substantial official reporting, but not yet the same clean, longitudinal, public firm-adoption series that the U.S. and EU now have. citeturn37search0turn37search3turn40search3turn20search10turn32search0turn24search4

### Data sources

For the Atlas, the highest-value recurring sources are straightforward. On frontier activity and China/U.S. comparison, Stanford HAI’s AI Index remains the best cross-country public source for notable models, model-performance trends, publications, citations, patents, and investment direction. For robotics and manufacturing automation, the International Federation of Robotics is indispensable, especially for robot density, annual installations, and operational stock. For electricity and data-center load, DOE, FERC, IEA, EMA Singapore, BMWK Germany, and related official energy agencies are the most reliable sources. For enterprise AI use, prioritize U.S. Census BTOS, Eurostat, Destatis, Singapore IMDA, and Korea’s enterprise informatization statistics where intensity data are available. For public-sector AI and procurement, the key sources are OMB and GSA in the United States, MOIS and MOEF in Korea, the Japan Digital Agency, Singapore’s GovTech/PSD material, and MeitY/MoSPI in India. citeturn33search1turn30search6turn38search1turn38search11turn33search2turn21search1turn29search8turn37search0turn40search3turn35view1turn39search10turn35view2turn35view5turn22search1turn23search11

### What the United States likely does better than China

The United States likely still does better than China in three areas that matter disproportionately for frontier-led conversion. The first is the **frontier model ecosystem itself**: Stanford’s AI Index says the U.S. still produces more top-tier or notable models, even though the quality gap has narrowed dramatically and Chinese models have reached near parity on several benchmarks. The second is **capital-market depth and vendor diversity**, which is harder to capture in one indicator but is reflected in the continuing U.S. dominance in frontier labs, cloud ecosystems, and commercial suppliers feeding both enterprise and government demand. The third is **transparent institutionalization**: the U.S. has a more visible stack of open standards, procurement rules, and agency guidance through NIST, OMB, GSA, and DoD. That matters if the Atlas values not just raw deployment, but also the ability to scale trustworthy AI across many sectors under public scrutiny. These are partly inferential judgments, but they flow directly from the public evidence. citeturn33search1turn33search9turn37search8turn39search10turn39search3turn39search11turn26search12

The U.S. likely also does better than China in **global accessibility**. American models, APIs, and enterprise software are more deeply embedded in allied and multinational firms across this entire comparator set, from Europe to Singapore and India. That creates a powerful externality: even where the U.S. does not dominate local state policy, it often dominates the model and cloud layer that domestic firms actually use. That advantage is not fully captured by patent counts or robot stock. It is an ecosystem-distribution advantage. The caveat is that this is a qualitative inference from model and platform leadership rather than a clean official statistic. citeturn33search9turn20search4turn34view7turn34view5

### What China likely does better than the United States

China likely does better than the United States at **state-directed physical conversion**. IFR’s 2025 robot reporting shows China installed 295,000 industrial robots in 2024, accounted for 54% of global deployments, and had operational stock above 2 million; Stanford’s 2026 AI Index also notes China’s lead in industrial robot installations. The White House’s own 2025 AI Action Plan implicitly concedes the physical-layer comparison when it says U.S. energy capacity has stagnated while China has rapidly built out its grid. IEA reporting also suggests China will meet all additional electricity demand growth through 2030 with low-emissions sources, with extraordinary projected increases in solar and wind generation. Put differently: the U.S. still leads more at the frontier lab layer, but China appears stronger at converting strategic intent into physical rollout in factories and power systems. citeturn33search3turn33search11turn33search1turn41view1turn33search2turn33search10

China likely also does better at **mass industrial diffusion under strategic coordination**. The AI Index says China leads in publication volume, citations, and patent output, while IFR shows the depth of its robotics rollout. That does not mean China is automatically more productive per unit of AI spend, but it does suggest a stronger ability to align industrial policy, supplier substitution, automation, and infrastructure buildout at scale. For the Atlas, that matters because “AI conversion” is often less about the very best model than about whether millions of machines, plants, grids, and public services are actually reorganized around AI tools. citeturn33search1turn33search3turn30search1

### What remains unknowable or poorly measured

The biggest measurement gap is **productivity**. Most official enterprise surveys still tell us whether firms use “at least one AI technology,” not how intensively they use it, whether it reaches core workflows, or whether it improves multi-factor productivity after implementation costs. This is true in the United States, the EU, and most of Asia. The U.S. Census has already had to revise its AI survey instrument to better capture modern business usage, and MoSPI’s own 2026 knowledge-economy work says sustained productivity gains will depend on better measurement as well as wider AI diffusion. We are getting better adoption statistics faster than we are getting credible productivity statistics. citeturn37search3turn37search11turn40search7turn23search21

A second weakly measured area is **public-sector AI ROI**. Many governments now publish procurement memos, use-case inventories, and AI playbooks, but almost none publish standardized cross-agency outcome data that would let researchers compare cost savings, cycle-time reductions, service improvements, or error-rate changes across agencies and countries. Singapore’s AIBots case study is unusually concrete; most other countries offer guidance without rigorous cross-government productivity measurement. citeturn22search1turn39search19turn35view5turn35view4

A third gap is **labor adaptation**. Official statistics can tell us where AI adoption is rising and where certain occupations are projected to grow or shrink, but they are much weaker at separating augmentation from displacement in real time. This is particularly true in countries with large informal sectors, such as India, and in countries where reorganization effects inside firms are not publicly observable. Japan’s Basic Plan, Korea’s education push, and U.S. labor-planning language all show governments preparing for labor adjustment, but none of them yet provide a robust causal national accounting of AI’s net effects on wages, hours, job quality, or bargaining power. citeturn34view3turn13search13turn41view3turn25search11

The final major area of uncertainty is **military and security deployment**. Public doctrine exists in the United States, Japan, Singapore, and to a lesser degree Korea, but the actual operational depth of AI-enabled targeting, logistics, cyber defense, intelligence analysis, and autonomous systems remains far more opaque than civilian adoption. Any Atlas comparison on military conversion should therefore be treated as a lower-confidence layer than the civilian economy, unless it is anchored in openly documented systems, budgets, or doctrine. citeturn26search0turn26search1turn26search11turn31search5
