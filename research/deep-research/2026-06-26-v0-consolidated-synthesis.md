## Literature Map

The research clusters into eight usable evidence families.

1. Existing indices and adjacent scoreboards: Stanford AI Index, IMF AI Preparedness Index, Oxford Insights, Tortoise, AGILE, WIPO, ITU, World Bank GovTech, OECD.AI, DESI, Eurostat, UNIDO and national digital-adoption datasets. These are useful for inputs, readiness, governance, and industrial controls, but they do not directly measure conversion efficiency.

2. Conversion theory: absorptive capacity, dynamic capabilities, general-purpose technology, social capability, national innovation systems, management quality, and diffusion literatures. These sources justify the Atlas as a pipeline from access to adoption, deep use, outcomes, and distribution.

3. Firm and macro AI adoption: OECD, IMF, Eurostat, U.S. Census, Destatis, KPMG, Pew, Ipsos, and productivity papers show rising adoption, strong firm-size and sector gradients, and uncertain macro pass-through.

4. Compute and energy: IEA, DOE/LBNL, Berkeley Lab, NERC, PJM, ERCOT, NDRC/NEA, State Grid, RTE, METI/MIC, MOTIE and accelerator filings define the physical deployment boundary: energized sites, interconnection, firm power, cooling, water, latency, chips, and permits.

5. Manufacturing, robotics, and embodied AI: IFR, OECD, UNIDO, NIST, MIIT, METI/NEDO, Korea MSS, Catena-X, Acemoglu-Restrepo and World Bank sources show that factory conversion depends on robots, industrial data, standards, integrators, maintenance, skills, and local plant redesign.

6. China AI+ and local implementation: State Council, CAC, MIIT, NDRC, provincial plans, AI safety frameworks, scenario-innovation policy, public-service platforms, and outside analyses support a China conversion hypothesis in selected domains while also identifying chips, openness, labor absorption, and capital quality as constraints.

7. Adaptation and distribution: ILO, OECD, World Bank, Eurobarometer, Pew, Ipsos, KPMG, EU AI Act, OMB/NIST, Chinese complaint and filing rules, Singapore IMDA, India Economic Survey, and Korea/Japan policy sources show that social license, remedies, retraining, safety nets, and distribution must be measured separately from adoption.

8. Scenario and security literature: Brookings, CSET, RAND, CNAS, METR, Epoch, Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, DoD and Chinese governance sources support scenario analysis where frontier advantage, deployment advantage, security sensitivity, and coercion risk can diverge.

## C. 1,500-word Synthesis

The deep research outputs strongly support the AI Conversion Atlas as a distinct product, provided it does not become another AI readiness ranking. Existing indices are valuable but incomplete for this question. Stanford and Tortoise help measure frontier and ecosystem strength. IMF, Oxford, OECD, DESI, ITU, WIPO, World Bank GovTech and UNIDO help measure enabling conditions, governance, innovation systems, digital infrastructure, public-sector maturity and industrial structure. But none of these directly asks the Atlas question: how well does a society turn accessible AI capability into observed economic, industrial, scientific, public-service, security and welfare outcomes?

The theoretical literature gives the project a solid foundation. Absorptive capacity, dynamic capabilities, management quality, general-purpose technology, social capability, national innovation systems and diffusion research all say the same thing in different languages: access to a technology is not enough. Actors must recognize useful capability, integrate it into workflows, redesign organizations around it, diffuse it beyond frontier firms, and pass gains through to workers, citizens, regions and public services. The Atlas should therefore treat AI conversion as a layered pipeline rather than a single score. The essential distinction is not "who has AI?" but "who can repeatedly convert AI into useful action under real constraints?"

Several claims are safe. First, the current index ecosystem leaves a genuine gap around conversion efficiency and realized outcomes. Second, frontier access, conversion capacity, adaptation capacity, distribution quality and realized outcomes are analytically separable and should stay separate in V0. Third, AI adoption is rising, but the strongest recurring empirical pattern is shallow and uneven diffusion: large firms, digital sectors and already capable organizations move first. Fourth, compute is no longer a purely digital input. Data centers are constrained by local power, interconnection, firm supply, cooling, water, latency, permitting and accelerator access. Fifth, manufacturing and embodied AI are better measured through a stack of industrial robots, machine tools, sensors, industrial data, standards, integrators and plant-level redesign than through frontier-model quality alone. Sixth, social legitimacy cannot be inferred from optimism or adoption. Trust, acceptance, legal remedies, training, safety nets and welfare pass-through are different variables.

The U.S.-China comparison should remain hypothesis-first. The evidence supports a bounded claim: the United States remains stronger on frontier AI inputs such as leading labs, advanced chips, hyperscaler capital, global model platforms, research universities, open standards, and openly documented federal procurement and defense pathways. At the same time, China may be better positioned to convert "good enough" AI into selected real-economy domains, especially manufacturing, robotics, smart terminals, infrastructure buildout and state-administered public services. This is not a simple winner-loser conclusion. It is a domain-specific divergence. The United States may lead in frontier capability and global stack distribution while facing bottlenecks in grids, permitting, skilled trades, fragmented federalism and long-tail enterprise diffusion. China may move faster in state-steered physical deployment while facing chips, capital misallocation, local duplication, demographics, labor displacement, data openness and information-quality constraints.

Manufacturing and robotics should be the first sector module because the data are unusually concrete and strategically relevant. IFR provides hard indicators for robot density, annual installations and operational stock. The evidence points to several distinct conversion models. China has the strongest scale pathway, with very large installations, rising domestic robot supply and aggressive AI+ manufacturing targets. Korea has the highest automation intensity and a state-led smart-manufacturing upgrade program. Japan has deep robot production, precision engineering and shop-floor knowledge but slower diffusion and workforce aging. Germany has strong standards-led industrial interoperability, Catena-X-style data spaces and high robot density but slower scale and higher energy-cost constraints. The United States has unmatched upstream AI and advanced-manufacturing R&D but weaker robot density and a thinner domestic robot-production base. The module should show these as different forms of convertibility, not as a single hierarchy.

Compute and energy should be the first cross-cutting constraint module because it determines whether frontier capability can be deployed at useful scale. The key insight from the reports is that national electricity totals are too coarse. Conversion fails when a specific load pocket cannot deliver energized megawatts, interconnection rights, transformers, water, cooling, latency fit, permits and firm power on the timeline compute investors need. The U.S. has extraordinary capital and accelerator access but faces fragmented grid governance, regional interconnection delays, local siting fights and ratepayer conflicts. China has more explicit national siting and compute-power coordination through East Data West Computing and related policies, but westward siting cannot serve all latency-sensitive uses and still depends on power quality, balancing, water and frontier chip supply. Green-power claims should be treated skeptically unless backed by hourly deliverability or firm balancing.

Several claims require caution. The Atlas should not say China is "ahead" in AI overall. It should not say a U.S. frontier lead automatically yields durable national advantage. It should not treat smart-factory counts, AI+ targets or public-platform users as independently validated outcomes unless outside evidence confirms them. It should not treat AI adoption as productivity. It should not treat occupational exposure as displacement. It should not treat public AI optimism as consent. It should not treat expert commentary, Substack-style essays, LessWrong-style arguments or speculative scenario writing as empirical evidence unless they contain datasets or formal models. Such sources can shape scenarios and red-team assumptions, but they should be labeled expert commentary.

The largest missing data are in the conversion middle and outcome layers. We lack comparable cross-country measures of workflow depth, AI intensity, pilot-to-scale speed, industrial AI use, digital-twin maturity, systems-integrator capacity, OT cybersecurity maturity, public-sector AI ROI, scientific AI conversion, military deployment, and distributional pass-through. Existing data are better for inputs and adoption than for durable outcomes. The U.S. Census and Eurostat are useful because they provide recurring enterprise AI adoption series, but they still do not prove productivity gains. IFR is excellent for robots, but no equivalent exists for industrial AI. China's official smart-factory and AI+ figures are important but need independent validation and category tracking. India's digital public infrastructure creates huge distribution potential, but firm-level AI adoption and productivity conversion are still poorly measured.

V0 methodology should therefore be a transparent dashboard, not a composite index. Use five layers: frontier access, conversion capacity, adaptation capacity, distribution quality and realized outcomes. Within each layer, classify indicators as observed, official-claim, qualitative-coded, estimated or missing. Use source reliability tiers and keep source type explicit: official government claim, official statistical dataset, intergovernmental dataset, academic paper, think tank report, industry dataset or expert commentary. Use country-sector-module pages rather than a national ranking. For U.S.-China, show hypothesis cards: "frontier winner, fast diffuser," "bottlenecked deployment," "electron and fab ceiling," "open-model compression," and "security-first divergence." Let the user vary frontier lead and conversion constraints independently.

The V0 product narrative should be: frontier is not fate. The opening article can start with a concrete strategic puzzle: if the United States reaches the frontier first, what has to happen before that becomes durable national advantage? Then the product shows the conversion chain. A model needs chips and power; a data center needs interconnection and firm supply; a factory needs sensors, robots, standards, integrators and trained workers; a public agency needs procurement, data governance and remedies; a society needs trust, retraining and broad pass-through. China, the United States and the comparators are best read as different conversion machines with different bottlenecks. The Atlas wins if it makes those bottlenecks visible without pretending to settle the race.

## E. Top 10 Methodology Caveats

1. V0 does not produce a single national score.
2. Official targets and official outcomes are labeled separately from independently validated findings.
3. Adoption is not intensity, and intensity is not productivity.
4. Frontier model leadership is not broad national conversion.
5. National electricity supply is not local AI-ready power.
6. Green-power claims are not firm-power evidence unless hourly deliverability or balancing is documented.
7. Smart-factory labels and program counts are not directly comparable across countries.
8. Survey results are sensitive to mode, wording, sample coverage and cultural interpretation.
9. Occupational exposure is not job loss; distribution claims need wage, mobility and safety-net evidence.
10. Security and military AI conversion should be low-confidence unless grounded in open budgets, systems, doctrine or independently validated deployments.
