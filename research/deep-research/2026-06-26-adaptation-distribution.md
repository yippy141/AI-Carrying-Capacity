## Measurement bias and coding cautions

The largest coding mistake would be to treat high adoption or high acceptance as evidence of durable absorptive capacity. KPMGŌĆÖs global survey shows the opposite is possible: people may accept AI use while remaining ambivalent or unwilling to trust AI systems. Eurobarometer similarly shows that Europeans often support AI at work while demanding careful privacy and transparency protections, and JapanŌĆÖs official AI-policy review makes the same point in sharper form: weak confidence in existing laws can coexist with substantial AI awareness and use. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn12view0Ņłéturn19search2Ņłéturn31view0Ņłü

A second mistake would be to treat **innovation preference** as the same thing as **worker tolerance for disruption**. IpsosŌĆÖs 2026 results show that many publics prefer AI-driven scientific advancement to regulation protecting affected industries, yet the same survey finds respondents are split 50ŌĆō50 overall on whether workplace AI will help or hurt workers. In the U.S., perceptions are substantially more negative; in India, Singapore, and Nigeria, they are much more positive. The correct interpretation is that publics can be growth-positive and transition-anxious at the same time. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn27view0Ņłéturn29view0Ņłéturn29view1Ņłü

A third mistake would be to equate **occupational exposure** with **net displacement**. The ILO explicitly argues that job transformation is the more likely impact for most occupations because occupations bundle many tasks that still require human input. OECDŌĆÖs regional evidence similarly suggests that even where automation risk has been high, net employment did not necessarily fall overall; instead, new job creation often outweighed job losses, even though the newly created jobs did not always benefit the same workers who lost the old ones. Exposure measures are therefore essential, but they should be paired with mobility and earnings measures before being interpreted as a social-stability risk score. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn16view0Ņłéturn15view0Ņłü

The survey base itself also creates bias. IpsosŌĆÖs multi-country work outside the U.S. relies on online panels and related non-probability methods, with credibility intervals rather than conventional margins of error, while KPMG notes that emerging-economy samples tended to overrepresent university-educated people relative to the general population. Any cross-country Atlas should therefore flag survey mode and sample-quality risk directly in the metadata. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view0Ņłéturn11view0Ņłü
