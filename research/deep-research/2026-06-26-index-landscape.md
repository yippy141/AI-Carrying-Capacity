# AI Conversion Atlas and the Current Index Landscape

## Bottom line

The current landscape is crowded with strong **AI capability**, **AI readiness**, **digitalization**, **innovation**, and **governance** measures, but it still leaves a major analytical hole: almost none of the leading indices directly measures **how efficiently countries turn AI capability into realized public, economic, industrial, scientific, or strategic outcomes**. Most existing tools stop at inputs, ecosystem strength, governance scaffolding, or proximate activity. Even when they include deployment signals, they rarely separate **frontier capability**, **broad adoption**, and **realized outcomes**, and they almost never estimate **conversion efficiency**ŌĆöfor example, the amount of productivity growth, public-service improvement, industrial upgrading, scientific acceleration, or strategic advantage achieved per unit of AI capability or spend. That gap is where an **AI Conversion Atlas** can be non-duplicative. If it is designed to track the pipeline from **capability ŌåÆ deployment ŌåÆ realized outcomes ŌåÆ efficiency of conversion**, it would do something that Stanford, IMF, Oxford Insights, Tortoise, AGILE, WIPO, ITU, World Bank, OECD, and UNIDO do only partially or not at all. ## What the current index ecosystem misses

The existing indices cluster into five families.

The first family measures **AI frontier capability and ecosystem strength**. Stanford and Tortoise are the clearest examples. They are strongest on R&D, patents, model production, talent, infrastructure, investment, and national AI ecosystems. They are indispensable if the goal is to identify who leads in capability generation. But they do not systematically tell you who is best at turning those capabilities into economy-wide productivity gains, better hospitals, stronger manufacturing, faster science, or strategic leverage. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn14view0Ņłéturn22view2Ņłéturn21view2Ņłéturn14view4Ņłéturn28view0Ņłéturn29view0Ņłü

The second family measures **preparedness and government readiness**. IMF asks whether countries possess the structural conditions for adoption; Oxford asks whether governments can harness AI for public benefit; OECDŌĆÖs new index asks whether governments are implementing the OECD AI Recommendation and building the right ecosystem conditions. These are more policy-relevant than pure capability rankings, but they are still mostly about **preconditions, institutional readiness, and ecosystem arrangement**, not observed downstream performance. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23view0Ņłéturn24search1Ņłéturn25view0Ņłéturn27view0Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn19view0Ņłéturn20view0Ņłü

The third family measures **governance**. AGILE is the clearest case. It is valuable because governance capacity really does shape whether AI diffusion is sustained, inclusive, safe, and politically viable. But governance is still only one stage of the chain. A country can score well on governance frameworks yet underperform on industrial use, scientific acceleration, or public-service transformation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn32view0Ņłéturn32view1Ņłéturn33search1Ņłü

The fourth family measures **general digital and innovation foundations**. WIPO GII, ITU IDI, DESI, eGovernment Benchmark, DAI, and GTMI are all highly useful, but mostly as **enabling-condition** or **adjacent outcome** tools. They tell you whether a country is digitally connected, innovative, or digitally capable in government. They do not tell you how much of those strengths is specifically being converted through AI. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn38view0Ņłéturn38view1Ņłéturn15view2Ņłéturn39view3Ņłéturn15view4Ņłéturn41view0Ņłéturn41view1Ņłéturn6search2Ņłéturn11search0Ņłü

France Num, JapanŌĆÖs IPA DX work, KoreaŌĆÖs enterprise adoption statistics, and ChinaŌĆÖs city-level AI evaluations are closer to the conversion problem because they begin to examine whether firms, cities, and systems actually use digital or AI tools. ## Design principles for an AI Conversion Capacity framework

An AI Conversion Atlas will be most valuable if it treats conversion not as a single score but as a **pipeline**. The first design principle should therefore be **strict layer separation**. Keep at least four layers distinct: **frontier capability**, **deployment/adoption**, **realized outcomes**, and **conversion efficiency**. Existing systems often blur these together. Your Atlas should not. Stanford and Tortoise can inform the frontier-capability layer; Oxford, OECD, IMF, DESI, GTMI, and IDI can inform enabling conditions; sectoral sources can inform adoption; outcomes should be measured separately. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn14view0Ņłéturn28view0Ņłéturn23view0Ņłéturn24search1Ņłéturn18view0Ņłéturn41view0Ņłéturn15view2Ņłü

The second principle should be **domain-specific outcome measurement**. ŌĆ£AI conversionŌĆØ should not collapse everything into a single welfare or GDP proxy. The Atlas should separately track at least five outcome domains that match your project statement: **social**, **economic**, **industrial**, **scientific**, and **strategic**. That would distinguish it sharply from generic innovation and readiness scoreboards, which usually stop at aggregate ecosystem proxies. WIPO, UNIDO, GTMI, and EU eGovernment Benchmark show that this kind of domain structure is feasible; what is missing is the AI-specific linkage across those domains. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn38view0Ņłéturn41view1Ņłéturn42view0Ņłéturn11search0Ņłü

The third principle should be **separation of frontier from deployment**. A country that produces frontier models is not automatically a country that diffuses AI widely in SMEs, hospitals, schools, laboratories, logistics systems, or ministries. KoreaŌĆÖs official OECD-based adoption statistics, JapanŌĆÖs DX surveys, and France NumŌĆÖs SME barometer show why deployment must be measured directly rather than inferred from research strength or venture funding. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn10search1Ņłéturn10search2Ņłéturn9search2Ņłéturn9search6Ņłéturn11search6Ņłü

The fourth principle should be **explicit conversion-efficiency ratios**. This is the clearest route to non-duplication. For each domain, calculate something like **realized outcome per unit of AI capability/input**. Examples include industrial upgrading per AI talent stock, productivity gains per AI investment, scientific acceleration per compute or research base, or public-service improvement per GovTech maturity and AI-readiness level. Existing indices rarely do this. IMF, Oxford, Tortoise, Stanford, and OECD each provide denominator-like ingredients; your Atlas can add the numerator. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23view0Ņłéturn24search1Ņłéturn28view0Ņłéturn14view0Ņłéturn18view0Ņłü

The fifth principle should be **lag-aware and peer-aware comparison**. Inputs convert into outcomes on different timescales. Infrastructure and human capital may matter over five to ten years; public-service deployment might move faster; industrial productivity gains may lag by sector. WIPOŌĆÖs use of inputs and outputs in separate sub-indices and TortoiseŌĆÖs five-year window logic both underscore the importance of timing. AI conversion depends not only on AI-specific assets but also on digital government maturity, data governance, interoperability, firmsŌĆÖ absorptive capacity, energy, connectivity, and industrial structure. ITU, GTMI, DAI, DESI, and UNIDO are especially useful here. A country with strong AI labs but weak interoperability, low cloud availability, poor procurement systems, or weak manufacturing depth may convert less effectively than a moderately capable country with stronger complements. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn15view2Ņłéturn39view3Ņłéturn41view0Ņłéturn41view1Ņłéturn15view4Ņłéturn6search2Ņłéturn42view0Ņłü

## Why AI Conversion Atlas would be non-duplicative

AI Conversion Atlas would be non-duplicative if it does five things at once.

First, it should measure **conversion rather than capacity**. Existing leaders like Stanford and Tortoise mostly tell you who has the strongest AI ecosystem. AI Conversion Atlas should tell you who gets the most real-world return from that ecosystem. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn14view0Ņłéturn28view0Ņłü

Second, it should **separate frontier capability, deployment, and realized outcomes** rather than blending them into one readiness or competitiveness score.
