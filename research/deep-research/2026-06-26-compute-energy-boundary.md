# AI Conversion Capacity and the Compute-Energy Boundary

## Where the physical boundary sits

The central question for an AI Conversion Atlas is not whether a country can build better models in the abstract. It is whether model capability can be converted into deployed inference and training capacity fast enough to matter. In practice, that boundary is reached when incremental AI demand can no longer be turned into energized, permitted, cooled, and connected compute on the required timeline. The binding constraint is usually local and infrastructural, not national and theoretical: a system can have ample annual electricity on paper and still fail to deploy because a specific load pocket lacks transmission headroom, transformers, water, permits, or chip-qualified halls. The IEA’s latest global assessment makes this explicit: data-center electricity demand is highly geographically concentrated; a typical AI-focused data center now uses as much electricity as 100,000 households, the largest facilities under construction use around 20 times that, and roughly 20% of planned projects globally are at risk of delays unless grid and equipment bottlenecks are addressed. The same report notes that a large share of U.S. data-center capacity is concentrated in just five regional clusters, which is why “societal capacity” should be measured as conversion capacity in specific places, not only national generation totals. citeturn25view0turn25view2

A useful planning heuristic is to define **AI conversion capacity** as the minimum of six non-substitutable constraints: site-ready data-center capacity, energizable electric load, reliable firm or dispatchable balancing capability, interconnection and permitting throughput, water/cooling throughput, and accelerator availability. If any one saturates, deployment slows even when the other five remain abundant. This is exactly the pattern emerging in current official assessments. In the United States, DOE/LBNL estimates data centers used 176 TWh in 2023, or 4.4% of total U.S. electricity consumption, and could rise to roughly 325–580 TWh by 2028, or 6.7–12.0% of national consumption depending on the scenario. EPRI’s newer scenarios push the U.S. 2030 range still higher, at 9–17% of national electricity, reflecting the acceleration of AI-linked development in just the last 18 months. citeturn20view0turn18view2turn13search10turn13search0

That means the relevant question is no longer “Will AI use a lot of power?” It already does. The sharper question is **when infrastructure lead times exceed compute deployment lead times**. In advanced economies, the IEA estimates new transmission can require four to eight years, while critical grid components such as transformers and cables now face lead times that have doubled in only three years. Berkeley Lab’s interconnection tracking shows the median time from interconnection request to commercial operation has risen from less than two years in the early 2000s to over four years for projects built in 2018–2024, with a median of five years in 2023 for the sample available in the 2024 edition. When those timelines become longer than the investment case for a compute deployment, physical conversion capacity—not AI capability—becomes binding. citeturn25view2turn28view0turn28view1

## Compute infrastructure is now spatial infrastructure

AI compute has become inseparable from power density, cooling architecture, and network geography. A traditional data-center design guide from DOE still uses 10–25 kW per rack as an illustrative planning range, but the new ASHRAE/NEMA/PNNL AI framework describes AI rack densities as having already climbed from about 120 kW to several hundred kilowatts, with megawatt-class racks expected in the near term. Vendor data for current rack-scale systems underline the point: a GB200 NVL72-style rack is specified at roughly 125–135 kW of operating power and is designed around liquid cooling, dedicated coolant-distribution hardware, and much more demanding electrical architecture than legacy halls. In other words, “compute” is no longer just chip inventory; it is a site-and-grid product. citeturn38search3turn38search5turn38search11turn17view6turn20view7

This is why national compute infrastructure is increasingly organized through geographic concentration. In the United States, Northern Virginia alone expanded to 4,039.6 MW of total data-center inventory in 2025, after delivering more than 1 GW in a single year. CBRE describes it as the largest U.S. data-center market; Dominion told PJM that it served a 2025 coincident data-center peak of 4 GW and forecast data-center demand rising to 16.6 GW by 2046, while executed contracts as of July 2025 totaled 47 GW of capacity obligations at different stages. Across the Americas, Cushman & Wakefield reports 43.4 GW of operational capacity, with 93.6% located in the United States. These figures illustrate the basic problem: national leadership in compute can coexist with severe local scarcity in the dominant clusters. citeturn24search10turn31view0turn30view0turn31view1turn24search1

China’s national compute infrastructure is being built through a more explicit state-planned spatial strategy. The 2021 national算力枢纽 implementation plan placed eight national hub nodes in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region, Yangtze River Delta, Greater Bay Area, Chengdu-Chongqing, and the western provinces of Guizhou, Inner Mongolia, Gansu, and Ningxia. The design logic is clear in the text: coastal and urban hubs handle low-latency and edge demand, while western, renewable-rich, cooler regions prioritize non-real-time workloads such as backend processing, offline analytics, storage, and backup. The policy also set a rough latency design rule of about 20 milliseconds end-to-end for certain interactive services, which is why east-west rebalancing can relieve some but not all AI deployment pressure. citeturn33view0turn32view2turn34view1

China’s more recent official numbers show how fast this buildout is moving. The NEA reported that China had already built 42 “ten-thousand-card” intelligent-computing clusters by 2025, that national compute-center electricity consumption reached 170 TWh that year, and that this was already 1.6% of total national electricity use. It further projected annual additions of more than 100 TWh during the Fifteenth Five-Year Plan period and around 800 TWh of compute electricity demand by 2030, or about 6% of total electricity use. Those numbers imply that China’s challenge is not whether compute demand exists; it is whether western siting, high-quality power supply, water, and domestically available AI chips can all scale together. citeturn23search9

Chips remain part of the boundary, but in an asymmetric way. Globally, Deloitte estimates that generative-AI chips could account for about half of semiconductor revenues in 2026 while constituting less than 0.2% of total chip unit volume; wafer shipments rose only 5.4% in 2025 even as AI demand surged, implying that the sector is fighting over a small, exceptionally valuable slice of manufacturing and packaging capacity. For China, the chip constraint is even sharper because it is partly geopolitical: NVIDIA disclosed that the U.S. government imposed a license requirement on H20 exports to China in April 2025, producing a multibillion-dollar charge and illustrating that China’s compute conversion capacity depends not only on power and land, but also on access to accelerator supply and software ecosystems. citeturn39view0turn10search0turn10search16

## Power, interconnection, and permitting have become the decisive bottlenecks

The United States is now the clearest example of AI capability colliding with grid fragmentation. NERC’s 2025 Long-Term Reliability Assessment found that 13 of 23 assessment areas face resource-adequacy challenges over the next decade, and it directly linked the problem to the fact that projected resource and transmission growth lag what is needed to support new data centers and other large loads. NERC also stressed that most planned additions in the next five years are solar and storage, which are weather-dependent and therefore harder to rely on for meeting inflexible, high-load-factor AI demand without additional balancing capability. citeturn27view0turn27view1turn27view2

That problem sits on top of a slow interconnection system. Berkeley Lab’s queue tracking shows more than 2,060 GW of generation and storage capacity actively seeking connection at the end of 2025, with more than 10,300 active interconnection projects at the end of 2024 and only 13% of capacity entering queues from 2000–2019 having reached commercial operation by end-2024. FERC’s Orders 2023 and 1920 were explicit acknowledgments that interconnection and long-range transmission planning had become structural obstacles. They should help, but the official record also makes clear that reforms are still being implemented and are too recent to have fully de-risked AI deployment timelines. citeturn28view1turn11search0turn11search1turn12search3turn12search10

PJM is the most visible U.S. load-growth case. Its January 2026 load forecast says summer peak load is projected to grow 3.6% per year over the next decade, reaching 222,106 MW in 2036, while winter peak grows 4.0% per year to 204,650 MW. PJM explicitly adjusted multiple zones for data-center load growth, including Dominion’s zone. This matters because PJM is not facing generic electrification alone; it is modeling data-center-driven load changes in specific utility territories. Virginia’s JLARC study reached the same conclusion from the retail side: utilities may be able to assign many distribution costs directly to data centers, but generation and transmission buildout driven by data-center growth is still likely to raise costs for other customers, including a modeled $14–$37 monthly increase for a typical Dominion residential customer by 2040 in real dollars. citeturn29view2turn29view3turn20view5turn20view6turn19view2

Texas shows the same collision from a different market structure. ERCOT reported in late 2025 that it was tracking about 226 GW of large loads seeking interconnection, up from 63 GW in December 2024, with around 73% attributable to data centers. By March 2026, ERCOT said it was tracking roughly 410 GW of large loads, around 87% of them data centers. ERCOT’s own explanation for moving toward a batch-study framework is telling: the prior single-study process caused repeated restudies, invalidated prior results, and could extend timelines by years. This is exactly what “conversion capacity exceeded” looks like in administrative form. citeturn35view1turn36view2turn35view0turn36view0turn36view1

Permitting is not an afterthought. It is part of the throughput constraint. Loudoun County ended “by-right” data-center development in March 2025, so new projects now require Board approval. France has responded very differently: RTE and the French state identified more than 65 suitable sites, selected five “fast track” sites near 400 kV infrastructure, and had reserved almost 18 GW of data-center connection capacity for roughly 80 projects by May 2026. Japan’s METI and MIC launched a formal “watt-bit” process in 2025 to co-plan power and communications infrastructure, while South Korea had already strengthened rules allowing power service to be denied for 5 MW-plus loads when system reliability or power quality would be threatened and created incentives to shift projects outside the capital region. These examples suggest that the real divide is not simply between “more” and “less” AI ambition. It is between countries that are learning to co-plan bits with watts and those that still govern them in separate silos. citeturn15search12turn41view0turn7search0turn21view0turn7search2

## Water, cooling, and the gap between green claims and firm power

Water is a real constraint, but it is best understood as both a **direct** and an **indirect** burden. DOE/LBNL estimates U.S. data centers consumed about 66 billion liters of water directly in 2023, with hyperscale and colocation facilities accounting for 84% of that total, and it estimates the indirect water footprint from electricity generation at nearly 800 billion liters. That second number is crucial: a seemingly low-water computing campus can still embed heavy water use if it relies on a thermoelectric-heavy grid mix. citeturn26view2turn26view3

The literature also shows why national averages are misleading. A 2025 Berkeley Lab review finds workload-level water use can vary by more than 10,000-fold, driven by differences in grid water intensity, server efficiency, utilization, climate, and cooling-system design. The older Nature review of data-center water consumption makes the same point from another angle: the U.S.-level water footprint is small relative to total national water use, but local conflicts can still be acute because data centers are concentrated and often rely on potable water and/or water-intensive power systems. Virginia’s JLARC concluded that data-center water use is currently sustainable in the state, but is growing, and that some localities may need stronger planning and reclaimed-water strategies as buildout continues. citeturn40view0turn40view1turn37view1turn37view0

Cooling is therefore not a side issue; it is a core conversion variable. DOE notes that cooling can account for up to 40% of a data center’s total energy use. Newer life-cycle work in *Nature* shows that advanced cooling methods such as cold plates and immersion can cut data-center blue-water consumption by roughly 31–52% while also reducing energy demand and emissions. These technologies are rapidly becoming part of the physical deployment boundary because AI racks are too thermally dense for many legacy halls. A jurisdiction that lacks water, liquid-cooling supply chains, or permitted heat-rejection infrastructure may have plenty of nominal megawatts and still be unable to host frontier AI clusters. citeturn3search16turn40view2turn17view6turn38search5

This is also where renewable-energy claims can become misleading. Annual renewable PPAs, green certificates, or offset-style accounting are not the same thing as dependable 24/7 power for AI deployment. LBNL explicitly states that it could not incorporate many U.S. data-center PPAs, behind-the-meter arrangements, or carbon-credit mechanisms in its analysis because of insufficient facility-level transparency. At the system level, the IEA projects that renewables will provide a large share of incremental data-center electricity, but it also says dispatchable sources—especially natural gas today, and nuclear or geothermal where available—remain crucial. NERC’s assessment reinforces that point by warning that the current queue mix is dominated by solar and storage even as reliability risks rise. In China, State Grid commentary has been unusually direct that large-scale green-power use for data centers faces a basic problem: wind and solar are intermittent and “eat weather,” while compute loads demand high-quality supply. citeturn26view0turn25view1turn27view2turn23search2turn23search6

The result is a simple but important distinction for the atlas: **green power claims should not be treated as conversion-ready power unless they are backed by hourly deliverability, storage, curtailment capability, or firm balancing resources**. Otherwise an atlas risks counting paper decarbonization as deployable AI infrastructure. That error would be especially serious in regions where load is concentrated, water is scarce, or outage tolerance is near zero. citeturn26view1turn25view2turn14search2turn14search17

## China and the United States compared

China and the United States are both capable of building very large AI systems, but their infrastructure conversion problems are different.

China has the advantage of strategic siting and administrative coordination. The state can direct large and super-large data centers toward hub nodes, coordinate them with wind and solar bases, raise green-electricity targets, and increasingly treat “算电协同” as a national industrial-planning problem. The July 2024 low-carbon action plan requires improving layout, moving non-real-time workloads westward, raising average rack utilization above 60% by end-2025, pushing average PUE below 1.5, and increasing renewable utilization at an average annual rate of 10%; by 2030 it wants international-advanced efficiency levels. The 2025 green-certificate policy adds a stronger signal: green-electricity consumption for new data centers in national hub nodes should improve further from an 80% base by 2030. citeturn17view3turn20view4turn4search11

But China’s model has four constraints. First, not every AI task can tolerate western siting because latency still matters for many applications. Second, the westward strategy shifts pressure onto local water, transmission, and power-quality systems even where energy is abundant. Third, grid reliability for large, concentrated AI loads still requires better balancing, storage, and direct-supply arrangements than renewable nameplate alone can offer. Fourth, the highest-end chip stack remains constrained by export controls and software ecosystem dependence, even as China pushes domestic substitution. citeturn34view1turn23search4turn5search1turn10search0turn9search2

The United States has different strengths and weaknesses. It retains the deeper accelerator ecosystem, much larger private hyperscaler capital flows, and the single largest share of world data-center electricity consumption. But the grid is fragmented across ISOs/RTOs, utilities, state permitting regimes, and local zoning systems. FERC can standardize pieces of the process, yet PJM, ERCOT, Dominion, and local governments are all still creating their own large-load rules, batch studies, queue screens, minimum-payment proposals, or zoning restrictions. That means the U.S. has very high **innovation capacity** but uneven **conversion capacity**. It may continue to lead on frontier training and early deployment, but it is exposed to local bottlenecks, higher consumer-rate conflicts, and slower harmonization of siting with grid buildout. citeturn25view1turn12search3turn29view2turn36view0turn15search12

The practical conclusion for the AI Conversion Atlas is straightforward. **AI capability exceeds a society’s physical deployment capacity not when total national electricity runs out, but when the time and cost required to secure energized megawatts, dependable balancing, water/cooling, interconnection rights, and permits in the relevant cluster begin rising faster than compute can be economically deployed there.** Today, the clearest leading indicators of that condition are large-load queues, transmission lead times, firm-power gaps, and water/cooling readiness—not model benchmarks. In the United States, the failure mode is fragmented coordination across utilities, RTOs, states, and localities. In China, the failure mode is the harder problem of making westward, green-linked, state-planned compute both low-latency enough and high-quality enough, while also navigating frontier chip constraints. The atlas should therefore treat conversion capacity as a physical systems variable, not a purely digital one. citeturn25view2turn27view1turn23search9turn23search4turn10search0
