# AI Conversion Atlas Literature Review

## Literature synthesis

### Framing AI conversion capacity

The most useful way to operationalize the **AI Conversion Atlas** is not as a map of frontier model quality, compute stock, or patent counts alone, but as a map of the conditions under which **available AI capability becomes realized social output**. The closest established literatures do not use your exact labels; instead, they sit under **absorptive capacity**, **dynamic capabilities**, **general-purpose technologies**, **social capability**, **national innovation systems**, **technology diffusion**, and **state capacity**. Across those traditions, the core finding is remarkably consistent: access to a powerful technology is a weak predictor of realized gains unless firms, regions, and states can **recognize**, **integrate**, **reorganize around**, and **scale** it. Cohen and Levinthal’s original absorptive-capacity framework emphasized recognition, assimilation, and application of external knowledge; Zahra and George distinguished between *potential* and *realized* absorptive capacity; Teece linked adaptation to dynamic capabilities; and Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson argued that AI’s largest economic effects are delayed by the need for complementary intangible investments and organizational change. citeturn25search16turn19search6turn2search4turn13search16turn2search14

That immediately suggests a workable definition. **AI conversion capacity** can be treated as a layered societal capability with at least five stages: **access** to relevant AI systems and infrastructure; **organizational absorption** of those systems into workflows; **diffusion** across firms, sectors, regions, and public agencies; **domain-specific conversion** into productivity, scientific, bureaucratic, or military outputs; and finally **distributional pass-through** into wages, service quality, resilience, and welfare. OECD work on AI as a production technology, IMF/OECD macro exercises, UNCTAD’s 2025 inclusive-AI framework, and Jeffrey Ding’s diffusion-centered theory of power all point in this direction, even though they use different language. A society with strong frontier access but weak managerial quality, low skills, poor data systems, fragmented procurement, and weak labor-market adjustment may look “AI rich” on paper while remaining “AI poor” in realized outcomes. citeturn17search2turn17search7turn30search0turn21search5turn23view0

This distinction matters because your project’s central question is fundamentally a **conversion** question rather than an **invention** question. The relevant dependent variables are not only GDP or firm profits, but also **state capacity**, **scientific productivity**, **military effectiveness**, and **broadly distributed welfare**. Existing literatures usually look at one slice at a time. Innovation studies explain why some economies absorb technologies more effectively; firm-level management research explains why some companies get more from the same tools; productivity research explains why gains appear late; labor economics explains heterogeneous worker effects; national-security research asks whether military advantage comes from invention, diffusion, or deployment. The AI Conversion Atlas becomes strongest when it treats these as **adjacent manifestations of the same bottleneck structure**. citeturn3search4turn20search0turn17search2turn22search0turn22search4

### What the main literatures imply

The absorptive-capacity literature provides the cleanest conceptual starting point. Cohen and Levinthal argued that prior related knowledge and internal learning effort shape whether outside knowledge can be productively used. Zahra and George refined this into acquisition and assimilation on one side, transformation and exploitation on the other. For the AI Conversion Atlas, this means the relevant question is not only whether a firm or country *has access* to a frontier model, but whether it has the domain knowledge, engineers, data stewards, compliance mechanisms, product owners, and frontline decision-rights needed to **translate AI outputs into operational routines**. Mason, Rincon-Aznar, and Venturini push the point further by showing that the skills associated with absorptive capacity are not just elite research skills: high-skilled workers matter disproportionately for identifying useful knowledge, but **intermediate technical skills** are crucial for applying it. That is important for an atlas intended to compare societies. A country may have strong AI labs and universities but weak deployment because the mid-skill implementation layer is too thin. citeturn25search16turn19search6turn25search1turn26view0

Dynamic-capabilities and organizational-capital research adds the missing implementation mechanism. Teece’s framework emphasizes sensing, seizing, and transforming; Brynjolfsson and Hitt showed that IT productivity depended less on hardware itself than on complementary organizational transformation; Bloom and Van Reenen’s management research found large cross-firm and cross-country differences in managerial quality that strongly correlate with productivity; and Bloom, Sadun, and Van Reenen showed that U.S. firms historically used IT more effectively because of stronger organizational and managerial complements. For AI, the implication is stark: **conversion capacity is partly managerial technology**. The same model, same cloud access, and same API can generate very different outcomes depending on whether an organization can redesign workflows, codify tacit knowledge, decentralize decisions where appropriate, and continuously measure errors and benefits. This is why “AI adoption” by checkbox is such a weak metric. What matters is **workflow redesign depth**, not nominal tool access alone. citeturn2search4turn19search2turn20search0turn2search7

The general-purpose-technology literature explains why these complementarities are so important. Bresnahan and Trajtenberg described GPTs as technologies with broad applicability, ongoing technical improvement, and strong complementarities with downstream innovation. Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson applied that lens directly to AI, arguing that the “modern productivity paradox” is partly a timing story: gains are delayed because firms must build complementary intangible capital before measurable productivity rises. Their later “productivity J-curve” work sharpens the mechanism: during the buildout phase, measured productivity can understate real progress because resources are going into training, data cleaning, software integration, process redesign, and experimentation that national accounts treat imperfectly. For your project, this implies that low near-term aggregate productivity gains do **not** necessarily falsify high AI potential. They may instead indicate that a society is spending heavily on complements without yet diffusing them widely enough for macro data to register. A Conversion Atlas should therefore distinguish between **latent conversion investment** and **realized conversion output**. citeturn0search18turn13search16turn2search14turn17search3

The “social capability” and national-innovation-systems literatures scale this argument from firms to whole societies. Abramovitz’s famous “social capability” idea held that catching up depends not only on available technology but on educational, institutional, and organizational preconditions for using it. Nelson’s national-innovation-systems work shifted attention from isolated R&D labs to the relationships among firms, universities, finance, labor markets, and the state. Comin and Mestieri’s diffusion work reinforces the point by showing that the question is not simply whether technologies have arrived, but how deeply and productively they are used. OECD frontier-firm research likewise emphasizes that the key problem in many economies is not a lack of innovation at the frontier, but **weak diffusion from frontier to laggard firms**. That distinction is central to AI now. Societies may excel at frontier-model research while failing to spread usable AI into small firms, public administration, provincial universities, or regulated sectors like health and infrastructure. citeturn0search3turn3search4turn3search15turn1search5

World Bank work on the “innovation paradox” and firm-level technology adoption helps translate those ideas into measurable bottlenecks. Cirera and Maloney argue that many economies overemphasize invention while underinvesting in the capabilities that let firms adopt and adapt existing technologies. The World Bank’s firm-level technology-adoption work “opens the black box” by measuring adoption at the level of business functions, not just whether a firm owns a given piece of capital. That is highly relevant for AI because a country’s conversion capacity may depend less on raw AI spending than on whether AI is embedded in **sales, logistics, procurement, HR, quality control, maintenance, scientific instrumentation, and state service delivery**. The right benchmark, then, is not “Do firms use AI?” but “How many economically important tasks are performed at a more advanced AI-enabled frontier?” citeturn14search13turn14search2turn17search13turn1search12

State-capacity and industrial-policy literatures widen the lens further. Khemani’s formulation of state capacity focuses on the state’s ability to provide public goods and implement collective goals. UNCTAD’s 2025 AI report argues that countries’ preparedness depends heavily on infrastructure, data, and skills, while OECD industrial-policy work emphasizes that modern industrial policy now includes regulation, skills, infrastructure, and technology diffusion rather than just subsidies to inventors. The U.S. Department of Defense’s 2023 AI Adoption Strategy is particularly telling because it explicitly frames the challenge as **diffusion across the enterprise**, not mere invention: it places trusted data at the base of an “AI hierarchy of needs,” highlights digital talent as an enabling layer, and calls for outcome-focused feedback loops rather than one-off procurement. In other words, even in a high-resource defense bureaucracy, leaders are treating AI advantage as an organizational-adaptation and deployment problem. That is a strong clue for your broader state-capacity dimension. citeturn15search20turn30search8turn6search13turn32view0

### Domain-specific implications

At the firm and sector level, the empirical AI-adoption literature already shows why your “conversion” framing is more informative than a simple “race” framing. Official statistics point in the same direction. OECD reported that, across member countries with data, **20.2% of firms used AI in 2025**, up from 14.2% in 2024 and 8.7% in 2023, with **52.0% of large firms** using AI versus **17.4% of small firms**. Eurostat reported that **19.95% of EU enterprises** used AI technologies in 2025, with **55.03% of large enterprises** doing so and especially high use in ICT and professional/scientific services. The Federal Reserve, synthesizing U.S. public surveys, reported that roughly **18% of firms** had adopted AI by year-end 2025. The UK ONS reported that around **25% of businesses** were using some form of AI in late December 2025. Yet the ECB’s SAFE survey underscores the difference between adoption and deepening: more than **70% of euro-area firms** reported some AI use in late 2025, but only **7%** reported intensive use. These are not directly comparable league-table statistics because they use different definitions and survey frames, but directionally they all show the same pattern: **use is spreading fast, depth remains shallow, and size/sector gaps are still large**. citeturn34view0turn33view1turn33view0turn33view2turn33view3

Micro-level productivity evidence is promising but heterogeneous in precisely the way a conversion framework would predict. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond found that generative AI assistance raised customer-support productivity by **15% on average**, with much larger gains for less-experienced workers. Noy and Zhang found that ChatGPT improved speed and quality in professional writing tasks and compressed the performance distribution. German evidence on formal and informal workplace diffusion suggests that individual, informal experimentation broadens the user base, but employer-led adoption determines whether AI becomes deeply embedded in daily work. Korean evidence similarly points to an “equalizing effect,” with larger time-saving benefits for less-experienced workers. Put differently, AI often works first as a **knowledge-transfer and variance-compression technology** before it shows up as a sweeping macro miracle. That is precisely the sort of pattern the Atlas should capture: not just average output gains, but where AI reduces skill bottlenecks, who benefits first, and what kinds of organizational settings translate capability into repeatable performance. citeturn4search12turn4search1turn12search15turn27view3

At the macro level, however, the literature remains more cautious. OECD’s G7 modeling and IMF’s Europe work both suggest that medium-term aggregate gains are likely to be **real but modest** unless adoption broadens and complementary adjustment proceeds quickly. IMF estimates for Europe place gains at about **1% cumulatively over five years** under current assumptions, which is meaningful but far below the more expansive claims attached to AI discourse. This is not evidence against AI significance. It is evidence that the binding constraint is conversion, not raw technical possibility. In Atlas terms, societies should be expected to differ not only in *AI exposure* but in how quickly they move from **pilot uses** to **process redesign**, from **infrequent use** to **intensive use**, and from **firm-level wins** to **economy-wide diffusion**. That also bears directly on welfare. Gains may remain concentrated in large firms, high-skill sectors, and already-digitized regions unless public policy widens access to the complements that smaller firms and weaker regions lack. citeturn17search7turn33view4turn17search2turn30search0

The science dimension is one of the clearest places where “conversion capacity” adds value. OECD argues that raising the productivity of research may be one of AI’s most valuable uses, but also stresses that AI’s potential in science is far from fully realized. The Royal Society’s *Science in the age of AI* reaches a similar conclusion: AI is already changing scientific methods, but gains depend on preparedness in research systems, data access, compute access, domain expertise, standards, and institutional trust. This suggests that scientific output should not be proxied by AI-paper counts alone. A country may produce many AI papers while failing to convert AI into faster discovery in chemistry, materials, biology, energy, or climate science. A better approach is to ask whether national research systems have the **infrastructural and organizational complements** needed for AI to raise discovery rates, reproducibility, and cross-disciplinary collaboration. citeturn16search14turn16search5

The military literature maps closely onto the same logic. Ding and Dafoe’s “general-purpose military transformation” framework argues that GPTs such as electricity—and by analogy AI—affect military effectiveness in ways that are **broad, delayed, and mediated by industrial productivity spillovers**, not just by narrow battlefield applications. Horowitz’s adoption-capacity theory similarly stresses that diffusion barriers, organizational adaptation, and the cost structure of militarily relevant AI matter at least as much as being first to innovate. RAND’s 2025 argument for an “AI diffusion marathon” and the DoD’s own 2023 adoption strategy both point to a deployment-centric view: data quality, talent, software delivery, feedback loops, and enterprise integration are decisive. The Atlas should therefore distinguish **frontier advantage** from **deployment advantage**. A state can lead on benchmark performance yet lag on fielded systems, acquisition speed, operator trust, logistics integration, or decision-cycle compression. Militaries, like firms, do not automatically convert model capability into operational advantage. citeturn22search0turn22search4turn23view0turn32view0

### Cross-language findings

The non-English literatures reinforce rather than overturn the English-language findings. Chinese scholarship is especially useful because it ties AI diffusion to the policy language of **新质生产力**. Zhang and Li’s provincial-panel study argues that AI positively promotes new-quality productive forces, with threshold effects: gains are larger where digital infrastructure is stronger, the digital industrial base is better, IP protection is moderate, and R&D intensity is higher. Fan, Meng, Bao, and Qu test the “productivity paradox” directly in Chinese manufacturing and find positive productivity effects rather than a permanent paradox, while highlighting mediating channels through labor quantity, capital-use efficiency, and innovation output. The strong implication is that Chinese scholarship sees AI gains as **conditional**, not automatic. It repeatedly points to infrastructural quality, industrial structure, and supporting innovation capacity as the real differentiators. That is very close to your conversion-capacity framing. citeturn27view1turn28view0turn9search10

Japanese, Korean, German, French, and Spanish sources show the same pattern in different institutional vocabularies. A Bank of Japan review explicitly treats AI as a GPT-like technology whose macro effect depends on diffusion speed, task exposure, supportive environments, and skill acquisition. Korean KDI analysis finds modest but real productivity gains from generative AI use and larger benefits for less-experienced workers. Germany’s federal economics ministry literature review emphasizes that AI’s economic effects are mediated by structural and institutional conditions rather than technology alone. French work by Aghion, Bergeaud, Bunel, and Delbouve highlights major sectoral and firm-size heterogeneity in diffusion, with large firms much more able to internalize AI production and use. Spanish evidence from Cotec shows AI use concentrated in larger firms and frames the central problem as how AI diffusion interacts with a long-standing productivity challenge. Across languages, then, the common lesson is this: **diffusion is heterogeneous, complements matter, and the implementation middle matters more than frontier rhetoric suggests**. citeturn27view2turn27view3turn10search2turn27view4turn27view5

### Novelty and limits of the framing

Relative to the literature reviewed here, the **genuinely novel** part of the AI Conversion Atlas is **not** the basic mechanism. The mechanism—technology plus complements plus diffusion plus institutions plus distribution—is already well established. Treating AI as a GPT-like technology with delayed gains is not novel. Saying that managerial quality, skills, data, and organizational redesign matter is not novel. Saying that state power depends on diffusion capacity and not invention alone is not novel either. Those claims are deeply rooted in the absorptive-capacity, GPT, social-capability, and diffusion literatures, and in the newer political-economy literature on AI and power. citeturn25search16turn0search18turn0search3turn21search5turn21search12

What **is** novel is building a single comparative product that treats these fragments as parts of the same atlas and measures conversion **across multiple output domains at once**. Most existing work studies one dependent variable: firm productivity, catch-up growth, scientific discovery, labor-market effects, or military competition. The Atlas becomes original if it does three things simultaneously. First, it separates **capability access**, **adoption**, **intensity/deepening**, and **outcome pass-through** rather than collapsing them into “AI leadership.” Second, it compares conversion into **productivity, state capacity, scientific output, military advantage, and broad welfare** using a common architecture of bottlenecks. Third, it makes distribution central by asking not only whether AI creates output, but **for whom**, **where**, and **through which institutions** those gains appear. That combination is not where the literature currently lives. It is a synthesis that existing sources strongly motivate, but do not yet supply as an integrated measurement project. citeturn17search2turn16search14turn23view0turn30search0

There is, however, one important limit. If the Atlas becomes only a renamed index of “digital readiness + AI adoption + patents,” it will mostly be a reframing of existing work. To stay novel, it needs to measure the **conversion bottlenecks that standard innovation rankings underweight**: intensity of use rather than nominal use, process redesign rather than software procurement, mid-skill implementation capacity rather than only elite STEM stock, public-sector execution quality rather than strategy documents, and distributional pass-through rather than aggregate output alone. Jeffrey Ding’s diffusion-centric theory of power, Brynjolfsson’s J-curve work, the World Bank’s task-level adoption approach, and the firm-level adoption literature all suggest that this is exactly where the highest analytical returns now lie. citeturn21search12turn2search14turn14search2turn17search13turn33view3
