# AI Conversion Atlas Manufacturing Robotics and Embodied AI

## Core finding

The manufacturing, robotics, and embodied-AI sector is not best understood as a race to build the strongest frontier model. It is better understood as a **conversion problem**: how effectively a country can translate AI capability into factory-level throughput, quality, uptime, flexible production, and export strength through complementary assets such as industrial robots, industrial data, sensors, machine tools, systems integrators, standards, and workforce capability. Across the literature, the recurring message is that AI’s gains in manufacturing are real, but adoption is still uneven and highly dependent on complementary infrastructure and organizational capability rather than algorithms alone. OECD finds that AI can raise manufacturing productivity through predictive maintenance, supply-chain management, workforce training, zero-defect production, and more capable collaborative robots, yet diffusion remains limited even in advanced economies. UNIDO similarly argues that industrial AI adoption is slow and uneven because of infrastructure, skills, finance, and data constraints. NIST’s recent manufacturing-AI work frames the bottleneck as reliable deployment at scale, with measurement science, interoperability, fitness-for-purpose metrics, and human-AI teaming all still unresolved. citeturn35view0turn30view5turn39view0turn39view1

For the Atlas, the most defensible high-level judgment is this: **China currently has the strongest scale-based conversion pathway; Korea has the highest automation intensity; Japan remains the deepest robot-production and precision-manufacturing ecosystem; Germany remains strongest in interoperable industrial data governance and industrial standards practice; and the United States remains strongest in upstream AI and advanced-manufacturing R&D but weaker in factory-level diffusion and domestic robot supply.** That means the “winner” depends on what is being converted into what: scale, intensity, engineering depth, standards-led interoperability, or frontier AI spillovers into production. citeturn10view1turn10view0turn34view0turn12search5turn32view1turn34view5

A second judgment is equally important. **Embodied AI is strategically salient, but today’s measurable industrial productivity still comes mainly from conventional industrial robots, machine vision, predictive maintenance, quality analytics, digital twins, and supply-chain optimization.** China’s 2023 humanoid-robot guidance and 2026 “AI + Manufacturing” action plan, Japan’s 2025 AI-robotics deliberations, and NIST’s 2025 workshop agenda on physical AI all show the direction of travel. But the hard evidence on current productivity gains is still concentrated in the older Industry 4.0 stack rather than general-purpose humanoids. That is an inference from the available policy and deployment evidence, not a quote from any single source. citeturn14search0turn15search1turn7search0turn39view0turn35view0

## Sector literature synthesis

The strongest empirical literature supports a two-sided view. On the positive side, robots and industrial AI raise productivity, improve quality, and lower some classes of costs. Graetz and Michaels, using data from 17 countries and multiple industries, estimate that increased robot use contributed about **0.36 percentage points to annual labor-productivity growth**, while also raising total factor productivity and lowering output prices. OECD’s work on robotics and production quality argues that industrial robotics matters not only for quantities produced, but also for the quality of what countries export and the kinds of tasks they can automate competitively. OECD’s manufacturing-AI reviews then extend that logic into the present, emphasizing predictive maintenance, near-real-time process optimization, digital twins, and AI-enabled quality assurance as the high-probability uses already affecting production systems. citeturn36view2turn36view1turn35view2turn35view0turn38view2

On the cautionary side, the diffusion literature is very clear that **technology does not scale itself**. OECD’s recent EU manufacturing chapter finds that AI adoption in manufacturing remains modest and fragmented: among EU enterprises with ten or more employees, the share using AI in manufacturing rose from **7% in 2021 to about 11% in 2024**, but the sector still lags economy-wide AI adoption. Skills shortages, data limitations, regulatory complexity, weak interoperability, and legacy equipment recur as the most common barriers. In the same chapter, OECD highlights that digital twins offer major benefits, but their full value is constrained by data quality, scalability, security, and interoperability challenges. NIST’s digital-twin standards work converges with this, centering attention on ISO 23247 and a broader standards roadmap for manufacturing digital twins. citeturn38view0turn38view1turn38view2turn38view3turn38view4turn34view3

The labor literature is also split, and that matters for an Atlas module because adaptation stress is part of conversion capacity. Acemoglu and Restrepo’s U.S. evidence shows that additional robot adoption can reduce employment and wages in exposed local labor markets; in their aggregate calculations, robot adoption between 1993 and 2007 is associated with a **0.18 to 0.34 percentage point decline in the employment-to-population ratio** and lower wages, depending on assumptions about trade and spillovers. By contrast, the World Bank’s 2025 *Future Jobs* report finds that in East Asia and the Pacific, robot adoption has so far increased overall manufacturing employment because productivity gains expanded output enough to offset some labor displacement, though the gains favored more-skilled workers and displaced many lower-skilled formal workers. The literature therefore supports a conditional conclusion: robotization can be employment-expanding at the sector level when scale effects are large enough, but it can still impose severe local adjustment costs, skill bias, and regional concentration. citeturn36view4turn36view3turn37view0turn37view2

That leads to a practical sector model for the Atlas. **Inputs** are the installed base of automation and the industrial ecosystem behind it. **Conversion processes** are the organizational and institutional capabilities that turn pilot projects into plant-wide or supply-chain-wide deployment. **Outcomes** are the measurable production and trade effects. **Adaptation stress** captures whether the gains are socially and politically sustainable. OECD, UNIDO, NIST, METI, and the Korea and China smart-manufacturing strategies all point in this same direction: the decisive variable is not whether firms can run an AI demo, but whether they can redesign workflows, assure safety and quality, maintain equipment, share trusted data, and generalize deployment across heterogeneous plants and suppliers. citeturn35view0turn30view5turn39view0turn30view1turn31view0turn15search1

## Indicator catalog for manufacturing and robotics

### Inputs

A manufacturing conversion module should begin with a hard-stock layer. The most valuable indicators are: **industrial robot density**; **annual robot installations**; **operational robot stock**; **manufacturing value added**; **medium- and high-tech manufacturing share**; **manufacturing and industrial value added per worker**; **capital expenditure in manufacturing**; **machine-tool ecosystem depth**; **semiconductor and industrial-control hardware capacity**; **engineering and technician workforce**; **systems integrator density**; and **IoT or industrial-data readiness**. IFR is the key source for robot density, installations, stock, and some production/trade data; UNIDO and the World Bank cover value added and industrial structure; national statistical agencies and industry associations fill in capex, labor productivity, and capital-goods depth. OECD’s IoT measurement work is useful for the sensor and industrial-data layer because it explicitly links manufacturing IoT adoption to productivity effects and measurement practice. citeturn11search15turn16search1turn17search0turn17search5turn35view4

For robot-specific inputs, the latest IFR data are especially powerful because they are internationally standardized. In 2024, robot density reached **1,220** in Korea, **567** in China, **449** in Germany, **446** in Japan, and **307** in the United States. The same 2024 IFR release shows annual installations of **295,045** in China, **44,453** in Japan, **34,164** in the United States, **30,596** in Korea, and **26,982** in Germany. IFR also reports that the top five markets—China, Japan, the United States, Korea, and Germany—accounted for **80% of global industrial-robot installations** in 2024. These are indispensable Atlas indicators because they proxy both the physical automation base and the speed at which firms continue to invest. citeturn10view1turn10view0turn10view3turn12search15

### Conversion process

The middle layer should capture whether installed capability actually converts into industrial advantage. The most important process indicators are: **pilot-to-scale speed**; **factory redesign capability**; **interoperability and data-sharing standards**; **MES/ERP/PLM and digital-thread integration**; **digital-twin maturity**; **maintenance and field-service capability**; **AI safety and quality-assurance processes**; **cybersecurity for factory systems**; and **supplier onboarding into shared data environments**. These are harder to measure than robot density, but the literature makes clear that they are the decisive bottleneck. OECD emphasizes data access, interoperability, cloud/HPC access, and skills as leading barriers; NIST emphasizes measurement science, reliability, and human-AI teaming; Japanese and German factory-policy documents emphasize security, interoperability, and factory-wide transformation rather than point solutions. citeturn38view2turn38view3turn38view4turn39view0turn39view1turn39view2turn30view1turn32view1

For this layer, the best practice is to treat several indicators as **structured proxies**. Pilot-to-scale speed can be proxied by the time from proof-of-concept to plant-wide deployment, or, in supply-chain settings, by the time required to onboard a new supplier into a shared data space. Interoperability can be proxied by adoption of standards such as **ISO 23247** for digital twins, **AAS/Asset Administration Shell**, **OPC UA**, and participation in ecosystems such as Catena-X or Manufacturing-X-like initiatives. Maintenance capability can be proxied by predictive-maintenance coverage, maintenance spending structure, certified service networks, or training throughput for maintenance technicians. AI safety and quality assurance can be proxied by defect traceability, incident reporting, functional-safety procedures for physical AI, and OT cybersecurity maturity. This is an inferential measurement strategy, but it follows directly from how OECD, NIST, METI, and Korea’s smart-manufacturing strategy define the real barriers to scale. citeturn34view3turn38view4turn31view0turn39view0turn39view2

### Outcomes and adaptation stress

The outcome layer should stay close to production economics. The best indicators are: **labor productivity in manufacturing**; **manufacturing value added per worker**; **defect rates and first-pass yield**; **scrap rate**; **unplanned downtime**; **overall equipment effectiveness**; **lead-time and delivery reliability**; **export competitiveness**, especially in quality-sensitive sectors; **robot production and export performance**; and **supply-chain resilience**, including time to reconfigure production after shocks. OECD’s quality work suggests that robotics can support higher-value and higher-quality trade; NIST’s supply-chain roadmap and Manufacturing USA strategy highlight resilience and domestic ecosystem capacity; and IFR’s production-and-trade statistics show where countries also control the production side of robotics, not only deployment. citeturn35view2turn34view4turn34view5turn11search15

The adaptation-stress layer is equally necessary. It should include: **local or sectoral job displacement**, **retraining intensity**, **skill shortages**, **regional concentration of advanced plants**, **SME access to digital infrastructure**, **worker resistance or adoption frictions**, **industrial accidents**, and **cyber or safety incidents linked to connected factory systems**. The evidence base here is broad enough to justify a formal stress score. Acemoglu and Restrepo capture the displacement side; the World Bank shows why aggregate gains can coexist with unequal distribution; Japan’s 2025 *Monodzukuri* White Paper shows manufacturing labor shortages and training constraints; Korea’s smart-factory program explicitly incorporates safety goals; and Japan’s factory-security guidance shows that the cloud- and IoT-connected factory increases the surface area for cyber and operational risk. citeturn36view4turn37view0turn42view2turn42view0turn31view0turn39view2

## China, the United States, Japan, Korea, and Germany

### China

China currently has the clearest **scale-conversion story**. IFR reports that China accounted for **54% of global industrial-robot deployments in 2024**, installed **295,045** industrial robots that year, exceeded **2 million units of operational stock**, and reached a robot density of **567** per 10,000 manufacturing employees. China’s robot stock represented **43.5%** of the world total in 2024, far ahead of every other country. IFR also reports that, for the first time, Chinese robot makers captured a majority of their home market, reaching **57% domestic market share** in 2024, up sharply from roughly 28% a decade earlier. That combination—massive factory demand plus rising local supplier share—is exactly the kind of input structure associated with strong industrial conversion. citeturn10view1turn10view0turn10view3turn12search3turn12search6turn12search9

China’s policy stack is also unusually explicit. The 2023 humanoid-robot guidance treats humanoids as a future disruptive product and frames development around AI “brain,” motion-control “cerebellum,” sensors, lightweight structures, and manufacturing-system integration. The 2026 “AI + Manufacturing” action plan sets concrete targets for **3–5 general large models deeply applied in manufacturing**, **1,000 industrial agents**, **100 high-quality industrial datasets**, **500 typical application scenarios**, and expanded safety-governance capacity by 2027. A related 2025–2028 industrial-internet and AI integration plan calls for at least **50,000 enterprises** to upgrade industrial networks and for high-quality datasets in **20 key industries**. This is unusually strong evidence that China is trying to solve not only the AI-model problem, but also the industrial-data and network layer that makes deployment scalable. citeturn14search0turn14search3turn15search1turn15search4

China is also building institutional pathways for scale-out. Officially relayed figures reported in early 2025 said the country had already built **30,000+ basic-level smart factories, 1,200+ advanced-level factories, and 230+ excellence-level factories**. By late 2025, the first **15 leading-level smart factories** had been announced, and some local-government reports in early 2026 cited even larger cumulative counts under updated administrative baselines. The exact totals should be treated carefully because the grading system is evolving, but the direction is unmistakable: China is trying to industrialize smart-manufacturing diffusion, not merely showcase pilots. citeturn33search4turn33search3turn33search6turn33search9turn33search11

The main stresses are also clear. The upside is scale, dense supply chains, and fast rollout. The downside is that embodied AI is being pushed into a labor market already under demographic and adjustment pressure, and the country still faces uneven quality in SME digitization and variable evidence quality on what “smart factory” labels mean in practice. For Atlas purposes, China should score **highest on scale, robot stock, smart-factory rollout, and policy coherence**, but with caution flags on transparency, cross-province comparability, and social adjustment. citeturn30view5turn33search4turn15search1

### United States

The United States has the strongest **upstream AI and advanced-manufacturing R&D base** in this group, but a weaker factory-diffusion profile. The 2022 U.S. National Strategy for Advanced Manufacturing places advanced manufacturing at the center of growth, supply chains, sustainability, and national security. The 2024 Manufacturing USA strategy shows the ecosystem moving further toward **digital twins for semiconductor manufacturing** and **AI for resilient manufacturing**, with NIST-backed institutes explicitly organized around those goals. NIST’s 2025 manufacturing-AI workshop focuses on reliable deployment, human-AI teaming, measurement science, and functional safety for physical AI, which suggests that U.S. institutions understand the conversion problem well. citeturn30view3turn34view5turn39view0

But the U.S. shop-floor base is thinner than its AI reputation suggests. IFR puts U.S. robot density at **307** in 2024, well below Korea, China, Germany, and Japan. The United States installed **34,164** industrial robots in 2024, remaining a top-five market, but IFR also notes that the **majority of industrial robots used in the United States are imports** because there are few robot manufacturers producing domestically. That means the United States can lead in AI software, chip design, cloud, and R&D while still trailing in the factory-floor hardware and automation ecosystem needed for broad industrial conversion. citeturn10view3turn10view0turn34view0

Recent productivity data add nuance. BLS reported that U.S. manufacturing labor productivity rose **3.2% in the first quarter of 2026** at an annualized rate, and by **1.5% year over year**, but it also reported that total factor productivity fell in **70 of 86** four-digit manufacturing industries in 2023 and that output fell in most detailed industries in 2025. In other words, the U.S. manufacturing base remains heterogeneous: excellent in frontier segments, less convincing in broad diffusion. For the Atlas, the United States should score **highest on frontier-AI complementarity and institutional innovation capacity**, but lower on robot density, domestic robot production depth, and broad-based factory conversion. citeturn34view2turn34view1

### Japan

Japan remains the most compelling case of **deep industrial capability with mixed diffusion dynamics**. On the input side, IFR identifies Japan as the world’s predominant robot manufacturing country and puts its 2024 robot density at **446**, fourth in the world, with more than **44,000** new industrial-robot installations in 2024. IFR also states that Japan represented **38% of global robot production**, giving it a uniquely strong position in the robot-supplier layer of the sector, not just as a user. METI’s 2025 AI-robotics materials reinforce the idea that Japan’s core advantage lies in manufacturing data, industrial robotics know-how, and the possibility of using tacit shop-floor knowledge to fine-tune AI systems. citeturn10view1turn10view0turn12search5turn7search0turn28search10

Japan’s conversion challenge is not hardware scarcity. It is organizational redesign under demographic pressure. METI and NEDO’s smart-manufacturing guideline explicitly argues that transformation must optimize the whole manufacturing process—including procurement, design, production, and sales—rather than automate isolated functions. Japan’s factory-security guideline further shows that smartification raises not only productivity opportunities but also cyber and OT risk, especially as data move into the cloud and across supply chains. NIST and OECD would recognize this immediately: Japan is treating conversion as a systems problem rather than an app problem. citeturn30view1turn28search0turn39view2turn35view0

The stress signals are serious. Japan’s 2025 *Monodzukuri* White Paper reports manufacturing employment declining slightly from **10.55 million in 2023 to 10.46 million in 2024**, persistent labor shortage pressure in SME manufacturing, a long-run decline in younger workers, and the fact that **more than 60% of manufacturing establishments** report a shortage of personnel able to train workers. That is the signature of a country that has world-class engineering depth but must automate and digitize partly because the labor and training pipeline is tightening. For the Atlas, Japan should score **extremely high on robot production, engineering capability, and manufacturing know-how**, but more modestly on fast diffusion and workforce renewal. citeturn42view2turn42view0

### Korea

Korea is the clearest case of **automation intensity**. IFR places Korea at **1,220 robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers** in 2024—by far the highest density in the world. Korea installed **30,596** industrial robots in 2024 and remains one of the five dominant global markets; IFR also counts Korea among the four countries that together produce **70% of globally installed industrial robots**. In conversion terms, Korea shows what happens when a country concentrates advanced automation in electronics and automotive clusters with dense supplier relationships and relatively uniform production environments. citeturn10view1turn10view0turn10view3turn34view0

Korea’s policy shift now aims to widen that strength beyond the frontier firms. The 2025 “AI-based Smart Manufacturing Innovation 3.0” strategy from the Ministry of SMEs and Startups targets **12,000 firms** for AI-centered smart-factory diffusion, seeks to raise the AI adoption rate of SME manufacturers from about **1% to 10%**, aims to build **1,000 manufacturing datasets**, promotes **AAS**-based data standardization for key equipment, and ties AI deployment to safety and labor-quality goals, including a target to reduce industrial accident rates by **20%**. That is unusually explicit evidence that Korea sees standards, data, and SME diffusion—not just robot density—as the next bottleneck. citeturn31view0

Korea also has a useful empirical baseline from its earlier smart-factory program. Official program reporting found average outcomes of about **30% higher productivity**, **43.5% lower defect rates**, **18.3% lower industrial accidents**, and **three additional jobs** per participating SME. Those figures are older and program-specific, so they should not be treated as national averages. But they are strong evidence that Korea’s factory-upgrading system has produced measurable operational gains and that automation there has not been understood as a pure labor-substitution program. For Atlas scoring, Korea should rank **first in automation intensity and very high in convertibility**, with the main question being breadth across SMEs rather than depth in leading sectors. citeturn26search2turn31view0

### Germany

Germany remains the strongest case for **standards-led, interoperable industrial conversion**. Its robot density reached **449** in 2024, third worldwide, and it remains one of the five largest robot markets and one of the major producer countries in the global robotics industry. IFR shows Germany as the largest robot market in Europe, while OECD’s IoT-in-manufacturing work and Germany’s wider Industrie 4.0 tradition continue to make the country the benchmark for connecting machines, suppliers, and engineering systems rather than simply adding more robots. citeturn10view1turn10view0turn35view4

The best current illustration is Catena-X, Germany’s automotive data-space ecosystem. Catena-X describes itself as a fully operational interoperable network that reduces costs through multi-tier data exchange across the automotive value chain. Its published success metrics are unusually visual and operationally relevant: quality-management use cases detect errors **four months earlier on average**, reduce onboarding of new suppliers to **five weeks instead of several months**, and make low-single-digit million-euro savings possible; a separate Koller Kunststofftechnik case reports **51% faster OEE calculation** and **15% less energy loss** after digitizing production and traceability. A publicly funded 2026 Data Space Accelerator adds compensation of up to **€30,000** for productive SME data exchange, showing that Germany and Europe are now explicitly attacking the SME onboarding problem. citeturn32view1turn25search0

Germany’s weakness is not conceptual clarity but scale-up pace under economic strain. OECD’s EU manufacturing-AI chapter shows manufacturing AI adoption still low across Europe; Destatis reports that **26% of German firms with at least 10 employees** used AI in 2025, but usage rises steeply with firm size, reaching **57%** among firms with more than 250 employees. That size gradient is exactly the pattern one would expect in a Mittelstand-heavy economy confronting interoperability, skills, and investment bottlenecks. Germany should therefore score **very high on standards, industrial-data governance, and engineering quality**, but only moderate on rapid, economy-wide scaling of industrial AI. citeturn38view0turn38view3turn32view4

## Candidate datasets and download instructions

The best dataset stack for this sector module combines one **core international robotics source**, two **core industrial-structure sources**, and a layer of **national and policy datasets**.

**IFR World Robotics** should anchor the robotics layer. Use it for robot density, annual installations, operational stock, customer industries, and selected production, import, and export statistics. The free press releases and executive summaries are enough for top-line cross-country comparisons, while the full *World Robotics* database is still the gold standard for deeper industry breakdowns and usually requires purchase. On the IFR report page, use the downloadable executive summary and country press releases for fast access; if you need sector-by-country disaggregation beyond what is in the releases, budget for the paid report. citeturn11search15turn10view0turn10view1

**UNIDO Statistics Portal** should anchor the manufacturing-structure layer. The portal provides free access to **INDSTAT Rev. 4**, national accounts, manufacturing trade, industrial production indices, and the **Competitive Industrial Performance** index. For this module, the most useful pulls are manufacturing value added, manufacturing share of GDP, manufacturing trade, and industry-level production data where available. In the portal’s data browser, select the database first, then country, industry classification, measure, and year, and export as CSV or Excel. UNIDO is especially valuable because it is designed for cross-country industrial comparison and explicitly standardizes manufacturing indicators. citeturn16search1turn16search7

**World Bank Open Data** should anchor the broad macro-structural layer. Use indicator pages and bulk downloads for manufacturing value added, manufacturing share of GDP, medium- and high-tech manufacturing share, and industry value added per worker. The relevant World Development Indicator pages expose direct CSV, XML, and Excel downloads from the indicator page itself. This is the quickest way to build a clean international panel, especially when you need a public source with transparent indicator definitions. citeturn17search0turn17search5turn17search2

**OECD and Eurostat** should anchor the digital-adoption layer for OECD and especially European comparison. OECD’s manufacturing-AI and IoT publications cite Eurostat series on AI use in manufacturing enterprises and give methodological context, while OECD’s productivity compendium is strong for harmonized productivity analysis. For Germany and broader European benchmarking, use Eurostat enterprise AI and ICT-use tables; for OECD-wide productivity normalization, use OECD’s productivity and industry datasets. citeturn38view0turn35view4turn9search16

**National statistical agencies and sector institutions** should then fill country-specific gaps. For the United States, use BLS for manufacturing productivity, BEA and Census/AIES for value added and capex, and NIST/Manufacturing USA for digital-twin and AI ecosystem metrics. For Japan, use METI, the *Monodzukuri* White Paper, JARA, and JMTBA. For Korea, use MSS, MOTIE, KOSMES, and program monitoring from the smart-factory system. For Germany, use Destatis, GENESIS-Online, VDMA/VDW, and Catena-X documentation. For China, use MIIT’s manufacturing digital-transformation service platforms, official smart-factory notices, and carefully curated provincial materials for case evidence. These sources are indispensable because some of your most important conversion variables—systems integrators, smart-factory maturity, AAS adoption, industrial datasets, OT security practice—do not yet exist in a single harmonized international database. citeturn34view2turn34view5turn42view0turn31view0turn32view4turn13search1turn15search1turn33search4

## Case studies and data gaps

### Case studies suitable for visual storytelling

A very strong China case is the Shenzhen intelligent-robot SME sample compiled under MIIT-related digital-transition work. In one published example, **Donglu Technology** used an industrial-IoT platform and digital cockpit to integrate ERP, MES, capacity, material, and equipment data; the reported results were **18% lower operating costs** and **25% higher production efficiency**. This is narratively useful because it shows the step between digital visibility and operational conversion, and it is visually easy to illustrate with a dashboard, line, and supplier network. citeturn24search2

A second China story can come from the first cohort of “leading” smart factories. Officially relayed reporting on the 2025 leading-level cohort says **XCMG shortened order-delivery cycles by 55%**, while **SAIC-GM-Wuling** achieved **24-model flexible mixed production** and **98.8% equipment utilization** in its “smart island” mode. These are excellent slide-ready cases because they link AI and digital twins not to abstract “innovation,” but to flexible production, cycle-time compression, and utilization. citeturn33search6

The strongest U.S. story is the **MxD adaptive-machining testbed**. Manufacturing USA reports that the system used inspection and adaptive CNC updates to push compliant parts toward **100% success** and cut **scrap by 95%**, with a rapid ROI. This is precisely the kind of industrial-AI story that travels well visually: scan, compare, rewrite G-code, rework only where necessary, and eliminate waste. It is also a valuable counterexample to the claim that U.S. manufacturing lacks practical AI conversion entirely. citeturn34view6

Germany’s strongest visual story is **Catena-X quality management and supplier onboarding**. Published Catena-X metrics report **four months earlier error detection**, onboarding a new supplier in **five weeks instead of several months**, and low-single-digit million-euro cost savings in some quality-management cases. Pair that with the Koller Kunststofftechnik case—**51% faster OEE calculation** and **15% less energy loss**—and you get a compelling narrative about why interoperable industrial data spaces matter for both productivity and resilience. citeturn32view1

Korea’s broad smart-factory program is also highly visual. Official program reporting attributes average improvements of **30% in productivity**, **43.5% lower defect rates**, **18.3% fewer industrial accidents**, and **three additional jobs** per participating SME. The strength of this case is that it is not a glamorous frontier-AI story; it is a diffusion story about the operational payoff of systematic factory upgrading at SME scale. citeturn26search2

### Data gaps and how to handle them

The biggest data gap is that **industrial-AI adoption is not measured internationally with the same rigor as robot density**. IFR gives clean cross-country robot measures; there is no direct equivalent for industrial AI, digital-twin maturity, systems-integrator depth, or pilot-to-scale speed. The correct response is to build a **proxy basket**, not to pretend the data are better than they are. For Atlas scoring, I would treat robot density, installations, and stock as hard variables; AI-use surveys, smart-factory counts, Catena-X-type participation, industrial dataset counts, and digital-thread standards adoption as soft but still decision-relevant variables. citeturn10view1turn38view0turn39view0

A second gap is that **smart-factory counts are often policy outputs rather than comparable operating measures**. China’s expanding hierarchy of basic, advanced, excellence, and leading factories is informative, but the categories have evolved and some reported counts reflect different administrative stages. Korea’s smart-factory totals are also program-defined. This kind of indicator should therefore be used as a **directional diffusion measure**, not as a pure cross-country performance score. When possible, normalize it with harder operational outcomes such as defect rates, OEE, downtime, or cycle-time reductions. citeturn33search4turn33search9turn31view0turn26search2

A third gap is that **systems integrators, maintenance capability, and OT cybersecurity maturity are crucial but weakly quantified**. Japan’s security guidelines, NIST’s measurement-science agenda, and OECD’s interoperability discussion all show that these are conversion-critical variables, yet public datasets remain thin. The best workaround is to assemble a composite from training throughput, registered service-provider counts, maintenance-program coverage, adoption of AAS/OPC UA/ISO 23247-like standards, and participation in industrial data-sharing ecosystems.
